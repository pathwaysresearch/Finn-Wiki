# Mechanism Design Applied

[[prof-bhagwan-chowdhry|Prof. Bhagwan Chowdhry]] applies a single intellectual toolkit across wildly different domains: **design the rules of the game so that self-interested agents produce socially desirable outcomes.** This page documents the toolkit in action beyond [[financial-inclusion-architecture|financial inclusion]].

---

## The Core Principle

Mechanism design is not about changing people. It is about changing the **structure** they operate within.

The insight from Chowdhry's PhD work (see [[intellectual-evolution|Phase 1]]): when actors have private information and conflicting goals, the design of financial instruments (or institutional rules) determines whether outcomes are efficient or wasteful. The same mathematics that prices currency swaps prices social impact.

**The pattern across every application**: Identify misaligned incentives --> design a mechanism (security, rule, institution) to realign them --> replace rigid structures (debt, tenure, mandates) with flexible ones (equity, exits, auctions).

## Domain 1: Security Design for Social Impact

### Investing for Impact (RFS, 2019)

With [[mentor-network-and-influences|Shaun Davies and Brian Waters]]. The paper that formalizes Chowdhry's mechanism design approach to impact investing.

**The fundamental tension**: Production of social good usually occurs **at the expense of profits**. If there were no tension, everyone would invest in social good. But wishful thinking ("we can have the best of both worlds") ignores reality.

**The false dichotomy**: Milton Friedman says firms should maximize shareholder value; Muhammad Yunus says businesses should serve social good without profit. Chowdhry's position: **both are wrong in isolation.** The answer is to design securities that make social outcomes financially relevant.

**Social Impact Guarantees (SIGs)**: The key innovation.
- Regular investors own the social business
- They sell a security (SIG) to socially conscious investors (philanthropists, foundations, governments)
- If the business **meets** social benchmarks: SIG holders receive low financial returns (but they got the social good they wanted)
- If the business **fails** to meet benchmarks: SIG holders receive high financial returns (compensation for social failure)
- Result: the business owner has a **financial incentive** to deliver social outcomes

**The counterintuitive element**: SIG holders *hope to lose money* (because that means social goals were achieved). If they get paid, it means social impact failed -- but at least they're compensated and can try again.

**FLII vs PII taxonomy**:
- **Free Lunch Impact Investors (FLIIs)**: Seek market returns AND social impact. In reality, they invest in projects that would attract regular investors anyway. They create no meaningful market impact.
- **Philanthropic Impact Investors (PIIs)**: Accept below-market returns because social impact offsets financial losses. They fulfill needs that market forces cannot satisfy.

"Being an impact investor implies a sense of altruism... What the term lacks is a definitive meaning." The distinction matters because FLIIs pat themselves on the back without actually creating impact that wouldn't have happened anyway.

### Design Principles for Impact Securities

From the research, the optimal design includes:
- **Pay-for-social-success contracts**: Larger payments when social goals are achieved (used in social impact bonds when profit investors partner with local governments)
- **Social impact guarantees**: Large financial payment to impact investors if goals are NOT met (incentivizes delivery)
- **Intermediation through large foundations**: Small individual SIG investors face free-rider problems (tempted to sell on secondary market to profit-seekers who don't care about social outcomes). Large institutional holders (Gates Foundation, Calvert Foundation, Acumen Fund) maintain incentive alignment.
- **Contingent social contracts**: Financial returns based on measurable ESG outcomes. Requires rigorous metrics -- "no securities are currently based on them."

## Domain 2: Sovereign Finance

### The Equity-Over-Debt Revolution

**"Greece Doesn't Need a Bailout -- It Needs Investors"** (*Time Magazine*, 2015)

The diagnosis: developing countries load up on loans from over-eager creditors based on rosy projections. When projections fail, they default. Governments bail out banks. Pundits argue. The same drama repeats. The pattern is "all too familiar and a bit too frequent."

The fundamental problem: **excessive reliance on debt** to finance public spending.

**The Uber analogy**: If Uber wants to expand, it can seek a bank loan OR sell equity to investors willing to share both upside and downside. "There are surprisingly few avenues for sovereign nations and investors to partner up, and there is no good reason for Uber and other start-ups to have more financing options than an emerging sovereign nation does."

**The proposal**: Equity-like sovereign instruments where repayments are **automatically** lowered in bad times and scaled up in good times. Examples: repayments linked to GDP growth, or commodity export prices.

**Historical precedent**: After Latin American defaults in the 1980s, old debt was swapped for Brady Bonds -- instruments whose value rose and fell with country performance. This was accidental equity, arriving only after costly default. Better to design it from the outset.

### Defaults and Interest Rates (PhD Work, 1991)

**Counterintuitive finding**: Borrowers who default face **lower** interest rates in subsequent lending.

Why: Anticipated exclusion from capital markets after default incentivizes borrowers to choose **safer projects**. This moral hazard reduction outweighs the reputational penalty. Additionally, larger borrowers face fewer alternatives and therefore default less.

**Implication**: Interest rates alone cannot measure creditworthiness. The incentive effects of default history must be modeled separately from the reputational penalty.

This is mechanism design applied to sovereign lending: the structure of the lending relationship (default consequences) shapes borrower behavior (project risk selection), which in turn determines pricing (interest rates).

## Domain 3: Institutional Reform

### IAS Reform (Times of India / Economic Times, 2020)

With [[mentor-network-and-influences|Prachi Deuskar]].

**The problem**: India's colonial-era administrative service creates entrenchment. 180 bright young officers selected annually begin with high human capital and idealism. But human capital depreciates, idealism wanes, some become corrupt, and lack of competition from outsiders exacerbates problems. "The system that was instituted by our colonial rulers to rule over us has now shackled us."

**The mechanism design solution**: Three attractive exit options after 7-year appointment cycles:

1. **Continue** in a prestigious senior position (but compete with lateral entrants from outside)
2. **Scholarship**: Fully paid admission to world-class programs (Harvard MBA, Princeton MPP, Chicago PhD)
3. **Startup capital**: Rs 1 crore seed funding from a professional VC fund for an entrepreneurial venture

**The cross-domain bridge**: This is the corporate governance technique of "golden parachutes" applied to public administration. "There is strong empirical evidence these have worked well" in the corporate sector.

The incentive alignment: Officers see their IAS journey as dynamic and rewarding, not a lifetime trap. Those who continue must compete with qualified outsiders (upgrading pressure). The system retains only those who remain fit for the job while providing attractive exits for those who would flourish elsewhere.

### University Governance (Times of India, 2021)

With [[mentor-network-and-influences|Deepa Mani]]. Written in response to the Pratap Bhanu Mehta resignation from Ashoka University.

**Core argument**: Financial resources alone cannot create great universities. Failed attempts in Singapore (three decades), Abu Dhabi, and Saudi Arabia demonstrate this.

**The Yale-NUS cautionary tale**: Despite Singapore's largesse, Yale-NUS never became a true counterpart of Yale. Students couldn't create political party branches. Events on dissent were cancelled. The Yale Daily News: "Instead of being in loco parentis, Yale-NUS operates in loco regiminis -- in the place of the state."

**The Merton Miller principle**: Prof. Miller, Chowdhry's PhD advisor, refused to attend conferences in Malaysia under Mahathir's illiberal regime. NYU Abu Dhabi struggles to attract top scholars despite lucrative pay. **Academic freedom attracts talent; its absence repels it.**

**Gerschenkron's framework**: Late-developing economies like India view universities as arms of the state, unlike the American "marketplace of ideas" model. This explains why simply importing Western university models fails.

**The mechanism design solution**:
- Private donors must **NOT** accept decisive executive roles (their fiduciary duty to shareholders conflicts with championing academic freedom)
- **Counterintuitively**, ceding power allows the university to flourish -- which is what donors wanted
- Create expanded executive boards: founding trustees + alumni-elected trustees + alumni fellows + university leadership + **faculty members**
- "Academics value academic freedom more than anything else... They must be given a central role."

## Domain 4: Technology and Market Design

### Land as Capital (2025)

**The problem**: Land near infrastructure projects appreciates as development progresses. Currently, insiders (politicians) receive illicit payments from developers tipped off about project locations. Developers acquire land cheaply, prices rise after announcement, corruption benefits a select few.

**The mechanism design solution**: An "equity-like auction model" where private players bid for the right to host infrastructure projects. Transparent, competitive, market-driven -- replacing politically driven allocation with a mechanism that captures land value appreciation for public benefit.

### Big Data and Discrimination (UCLA Newsroom, 2016)

With Sanjiv Das and Barney Hartman-Glaser.

**The insight**: Racial profiling, stereotyping, and discrimination are forms of **small data** prediction. We use coarse signals (race, gender, age) because we lack better data and must make quick decisions.

**The analogy**: Is that twisted shape a poisonous snake or a rope? Sometimes practical to assume it's a snake. But with better data, we wouldn't need that assumption.

**The big data promise**: Amazon's recommendation engine doesn't care about your demographics -- it predicts based on behavior. With enough data, coarse signals become redundant. "Big data has immense potential to trump stereotypes derived from small data."

**The mechanism design angle**: Design data systems so that prediction accuracy improves while discrimination decreases. More individual treatment, less group-based stereotyping.

### Automation and Work (2017)

**"Automation, not free trade, is the bigger threat to American jobs."** Robots carry no passports and don't need visas. AI arrives on our devices while we sleep.

**Solutions (all mechanism design)**:
1. Education vouchers for displaced workers' children (incentivize skill development, not industry protection)
2. Micro-finance loans for entrepreneurship (risk-sharing for the displaced)
3. Universal basic income (funded by reallocating subsidies that favor the rich -- agricultural subsidies, tax-deductibility of corporate debt and mortgage interest)

"Entrenched interests will fiercely oppose such moves. But the Trump-led transformation into a protectionist and parochial society, if enacted, would be far worse."

## The Meta-Pattern

Across all four domains, the same structure repeats:

1. **Diagnose**: Identify where incentives are misaligned (creditors vs. debtors, donors vs. academics, incumbents vs. innovation)
2. **Design**: Create a mechanism (security, auction, exit path, data system) that realigns incentives
3. **Flexibility**: Replace rigid structures (debt, tenure, mandates, stereotypes) with flexible ones (equity, exits, auctions, individual data)
4. **Communicate**: Make the counterintuitive result vivid through analogy (see [[rhetorical-style-and-pedagogy]])

This is the [[thinking-patterns-and-frameworks|incentive-first lens]] made operational across every domain Chowdhry touches.
