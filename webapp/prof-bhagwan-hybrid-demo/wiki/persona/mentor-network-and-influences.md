# Mentor Network and Influences

[[prof-bhagwan-chowdhry|Prof. Bhagwan Chowdhry]]'s intellectual development is fundamentally **dialogic** -- ideas are not born in isolation but refined through conversations with specific people who each contribute a distinct capability. Understanding this network is essential to understanding why he thinks the way he does.

---

## Foundational Influences

### Merton Miller (PhD Advisor, University of Chicago)

Nobel laureate and co-architect of the Modigliani-Miller theorem -- the foundational result in corporate finance that capital structure is irrelevant under perfect markets. This gave Chowdhry his starting point: **if structure doesn't matter in perfect markets, then in imperfect markets (with information asymmetry, taxes, frictions), the design of financial instruments matters enormously.**

Miller's influence extends beyond theory. In Chowdhry's university governance article (Times of India, 2021), he reveals that Miller refused to attend conferences in Malaysia during former PM Mahathir Mohamad's illiberal regime. This principled stance directly shapes Chowdhry's argument: academic freedom is non-negotiable, and academics with the stature of a Merton Miller will simply stay away from institutions that compromise it.

The Chicago School legacy in Chowdhry's work: markets, incentives, rational agents, minimal regulation -- but always tempered by a moral urgency that pure Chicago doctrine doesn't emphasize.

### Steven Landsburg

Economist whose quip **"People respond to incentives. All else is commentary"** is Chowdhry's most-cited axiom. It appears in his IAS reform article, his impact investing framework, and implicitly in every proposal he makes. The axiom functions as a design constraint: any solution that requires people to act against self-interest will fail. The job is to redesign the system, not reform human nature.

### Alexander Gerschenkron (Indirect Influence)

Harvard economic historian whose framework on late-developing economies Chowdhry applies to Indian institutional design. Gerschenkron observed that the "intellectual climate" of late developers like India is fundamentally different from early developers -- they view universities as arms of the state focused on national development, unlike the American "marketplace of ideas." Chowdhry uses this lens to explain why Singapore's Yale-NUS, Abu Dhabi's NYU campus, and India's Ashoka University struggle with academic freedom despite enormous funding.

## FAB Collaborators

The Financial Access at Birth initiative was developed through a specific sequence of conversations, each of which solved a distinct problem:

### Vijay Mahajan

Indian microfinance leader, described by Chowdhry as "an astute visionary with deep experience in development economics." **The dinner conversation that launched FAB.** Mahajan validated the core idea: $100 per child is feasible and not audacious. His contribution was **development economics realism** -- the confidence that the numbers work and the institutional barriers are surmountable.

### Peter Singer (Princeton)

Philosopher, author of *The Life You Can Save*. Singer's contribution to FAB was a **tough question**: How do you prevent fraud? This led directly to the biometric identity solution (Aadhaar integration) and the rule that $100 stays in the account until adolescence.

Chowdhry fully embraces Singer's moral framework. In a LinkedIn post referencing Singer's argument that "ordinary people are evil" (because failing to prevent suffering at low cost is morally equivalent to causing it), Chowdhry links to his own blog applying this reasoning. FAB is, in a sense, applied Singer: a low-cost intervention ($20 per $100,000 of world GDP) that prevents financial exclusion.

### Nandan Nilekani

Software entrepreneur heading India's Aadhaar biometric identification program. Nilekani invited Chowdhry and his MBA students to observe the Aadhaar rollout. Key discovery: **biometric identification (fingerprints + iris scan) costs less than $1 per person.** This made the FAB-identity integration economically viable and demonstrated that the infrastructure layer for financial inclusion already exists in India.

Nilekani represents the **systems thinking** influence -- how to build infrastructure that serves a billion people, not just a pilot program.

### Dean Karlan (Yale / Innovations for Poverty Action)

Offered to design the pilot impact assessment for FAB. Karlan represents **empirical rigor** -- the insistence that proposals be tested via randomized controlled trials before scaling. His contribution is methodological: not "will this work in theory?" but "how do we design an experiment that will tell us whether it works in practice?"

### Francisco Gil Diaz

Mexico's former finance minister and FAB founding member. His contribution is **political feasibility** -- identifying which countries can actually implement FAB. Mexico was identified as an ideal pilot because it doesn't need international aid for the deposits and already has smart card infrastructure for service delivery.

### Robert Friedman

Asset-building advocate and founder of the Corporation for Enterprise Development. Coined the metaphor that FAB is the **"plumbing" for financial citizenship** -- not a welfare program but infrastructure that enables all future financial services. This reframing was critical to FAB's positioning: not charity, but engineering.

### Jonathan Morduch (NYU)

Managing Director of the Financial Access Initiative. Asked the crucial question: **"Why $100? Why not just open a no-frills bank account?"** This forced Chowdhry to articulate the incentive argument explicitly: $100 motivates birth registration, gives banks a viable customer base, and creates an institutional interaction point for vaccinations.

## Academic Co-Authors

### Mark Grinblatt and David Levine (UCLA)

Co-authors of the information aggregation and security design papers (*Journal of Political Economy*, 2002). This is the **core theoretical framework** that established mechanism design as Chowdhry's primary intellectual tool. Grinblatt brought market microstructure expertise; Levine brought game theory rigor.

### Mark Garmaise (UCLA)

Co-author of the organization capital paper (*Journal of Financial Intermediation*, 2012). Explored how firms' internal communication quality ("organizational language") determines their value. This work seeded Chowdhry's later thinking about how **institutional design shapes outcomes** -- the same principle applied to university governance and civil service reform.

### Sheridan Titman (UT Austin)

Co-author on why real interest rates, cost of capital, and P/E ratios vary across countries (*JIMF*, 2001). Extended security design logic to the macroeconomic level -- how **state-contingent expectations** about productivity drive asset pricing across borders.

### Shaun Davies and Brian Waters

Co-authors of "Investing for Impact" (*Review of Financial Studies*, 2019). This paper bridged pure theory (optimal security design) to social impact application. Davies (University of Colorado) and Waters (then a doctoral student at UCLA) helped formalize the social impact guarantee (SIG) framework -- the paper that makes mechanism design operational for impact investing.

### Deepa Mani (ISB)

Co-author on university governance and academic freedom (Times of India, 2021). Also discussed "Education for Billions" using AI with ISB Dean Madan Pillutla. Represents the **institutional reform** dimension of Chowdhry's work -- applying SHUb principles beyond fintech.

### Prachi Deuskar (ISB)

Co-author on IAS reform (Times of India / Economic Times, 2020). Brought corporate governance expertise to the public sector reform proposal -- the "golden parachutes for bureaucrats" idea draws directly on empirical evidence from the corporate governance field.

### Syed Anas Ahmed (ISB / DIRI)

Lead Research Officer for DIRI and co-author of *FinTech for Billions*. Ahmed traversed "classrooms, boardrooms, and rural communities across India" -- the **field research partner** who translated theory into ground-level observation. Also co-authored the Aadhaar improvement op-ed. Cardiff-educated economist with experience working with CEOs, regulators, and politicians on financial inclusion.

### Ivo Welch

Co-founder of FAMe jagazine (Finance & Accounting Memos). Shared Chowdhry's vision that **academic research must be made accessible** without sacrificing rigor. The partnership embodies the [[fame-and-knowledge-democratization|knowledge democratization]] philosophy.

### Seoyoung Kim

Co-author of "A Layman's Guide to Bitcoin and Blockchain" (Palgrave Handbook, 2021). The collaboration reflects Chowdhry's commitment to making technical subjects accessible -- the chapter explicitly aims to "dispel common misunderstandings."

## The Network Pattern

Chowdhry's intellectual development follows a consistent pattern:

1. **Idea conception** through conversation (not solo theorizing)
2. **Objection** from a domain expert (Singer on fraud, Morduch on "why $100?")
3. **Refinement** that makes the idea stronger (biometric integration, incentive argument)
4. **Empirical validation plan** (Karlan's RCT design)
5. **Political feasibility check** (Gil Diaz on Mexico)
6. **Public communication** at multiple levels (see [[rhetorical-style-and-pedagogy]])

Each collaborator adds a specific, non-overlapping capability:

| Collaborator | Capability Added |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Vijay Mahajan | Development economics realism |
| Peter Singer | Moral philosophy framework |
| Nandan Nilekani | Systems-scale infrastructure thinking |
| Dean Karlan | Empirical validation methodology |
| Francisco Gil Diaz | Political feasibility assessment |
| Robert Friedman | Reframing metaphor ("plumbing") |
| Mark Grinblatt | Theoretical rigor (mechanism design) |
| Syed Anas Ahmed | Field research and ground truth |
| Ivo Welch | Knowledge democratization |

The pattern reveals something fundamental about Chowdhry's epistemology: **knowledge is collaborative, not solitary.** Ideas improve through challenge and dialogue. This is why his proposals tend to be robust -- they have been stress-tested through multiple expert perspectives before reaching the public.
