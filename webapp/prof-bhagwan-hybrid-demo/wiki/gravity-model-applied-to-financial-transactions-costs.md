# Gravity Model Applied to Financial Transactions Costs

## Core Insight
The gravity model of trade — bilateral flows proportional to economic mass and inversely proportional to distance — partially applies to financial markets, but requires fundamental reinterpretation. Physical distance is a poor proxy for financial friction; *informational distance*, *market depth*, and *regulatory barriers* are the true analogues.

## Three-Part Assessment

### 1. Information Asymmetry as "Distance" — Partial Fit
Gravity works in goods trade because distance proxies for information costs, cultural familiarity, and institutional trust. In financial markets, the equivalent friction is *informational distance*: thin price discovery, asymmetric information, and weak institutional quality. Higher bid-ask spreads for exotic currency pairs reflect informational remoteness, not geographic remoteness.

### 2. Liquidity Mass as "Economic Size" — Good Fit
Large, liquid markets (dollar, euro, yen) generate lower transactions costs because their "mass" attracts more counterparties. Chowdhry, Aliber & Yan (2000) showed inter-bank FX costs for a $100,000 trade ranged from just $18–$51 — and had fallen substantially over 1977–1999 — precisely because dollar markets have enormous gravitational pull. Market depth is the financial analogue of GDP mass.

### 3. Triangular Arbitrage Structure — Breaks the Model
Cross-rates (e.g., DM/pound) are derived from dollar rates, not independently priced. The bid-ask spread compounds multiplicatively:

  S^{DM/£}_{bid} = S^{$/£}_{bid} / S^{$/DM}_{ask}

This non-linear compounding means transactions costs across currency pairs do not simply add with distance. Frenkel and Levich erred by an order of magnitude by ignoring this structure. A pure gravity model — which assumes additive, distance-proportional frictions — cannot capture this.

## What Replaces "Distance" in Financial Gravity
- Regulatory barriers (capital controls, reporting requirements)
- Settlement risk across time zones
- Information asymmetry about counterparty quality
- Network effects in payment systems
- Market liquidity depth

## Implication for Financial Inclusion
Financial assets are weightless — geography imposes near-zero physical cost. Yet the poor pay the highest effective "distance" penalties via remittance fees, thin local markets, and regulatory exclusion. If bitcoin or mobile money captured 25% of the $640B remittance market and eliminated a 5% fee, that is $8B annually returned to senders — a gravitational collapse of informational distance in favor of the marginalized.
