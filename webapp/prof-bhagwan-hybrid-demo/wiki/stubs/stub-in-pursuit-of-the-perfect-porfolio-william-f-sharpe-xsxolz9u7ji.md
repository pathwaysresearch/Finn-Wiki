## Abstract

This is a long-form interview conducted by Andrew Lo (MIT) with Nobel laureate William F. Sharpe, as part of the research project accompanying the forthcoming book *In Pursuit of the Perfect Portfolio* by Steve Foerster and Andrew Lo. Sharpe discusses the origins of the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM), his doctoral dissertation under the influence of Harry Markowitz, and the founding of passive/index investing. The conversation traces the intellectual and practical history of modern portfolio theory from early computational finance through the creation of the first major index funds at Wells Fargo in the late 1960s–early 1970s.

## Key Claims

- Harry Markowitz served as Sharpe's de facto dissertation adviser at RAND, despite not being on the UCLA faculty, through an arrangement with Fred Weston and Arman Alchian
- Sharpe's dissertation was built on the "single index model" (or "diagonal model") of security return formation, which originated in Markowitz's work
- The first index fund efforts at Wells Fargo involved 100 equally-weighted NYSE stocks rebalanced monthly, which proved impractical and inconsistent with theory
- Sharpe's early computational analysis covered 96 stocks and would have cost $300 in commercial programming time on an IBM 709 (or 704) mainframe, though he ran it for free at RAND
- The transition from equal-weighted to market-capitalization-weighted index funds was driven by both theoretical consistency and practical implementation challenges
