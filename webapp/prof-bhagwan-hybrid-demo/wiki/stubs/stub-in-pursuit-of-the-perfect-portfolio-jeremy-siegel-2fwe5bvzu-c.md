## Abstract

This is a video interview with Jeremy Siegel of the Wharton School, conducted by Andrew Lo and Steve Foerster as part of their "In Pursuit of the Perfect Portfolio" book project (MIT Laboratory for Financial Engineering, 2018). Siegel discusses his intellectual formation, academic career trajectory from macroeconomics to finance, key mentors including Paul Samuelson and Milton Friedman, and his contributions to understanding stock markets, inflation, and monetary theory. The interview provides biographical context and Siegel's perspectives on long-term equity returns, portfolio construction, and the evolution of financial economics as a discipline.

## Key Claims

- Jeremy Siegel's PhD thesis focused on the stability of a monetary economy with inflationary expectations, building on a dynamic neo-Keynesian (Patinkin-style) model during a period of high inflation and interest rates (10-14%)
- Siegel was a classmate of Nobel laureate Robert Merton at graduate school and maintained a 50+ year friendship with him
- Milton Friedman served as a longtime colleague and mentor to Siegel, and Siegel dedicated a book to both Samuelson and Friedman
- Siegel identified a paradox related to the pound-dollar exchange rate involving expected values of random variables under risk-neutral pricing, which attracted attention from researchers for years afterward
- Siegel transitioned from macroeconomics research to finance around 1987, prompted by a New York Stock Exchange 200th anniversary book project with colleague Marshall Blume
