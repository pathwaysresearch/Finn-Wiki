## Abstract

This is a transcript from MIT 15.401 (Finance Theory I), Session 11, taught by Andrew Lo in Fall 2008. The lecture introduces option pricing fundamentals, including the mechanics of call and put options, payoff diagrams, and the intuition behind why options have value. The session is set against the backdrop of the 2008 financial crisis, with Lo noting that option prices (particularly S&P 500 puts) reveal market expectations about future volatility. The lecture serves as a precursor to a more technical option-pricing formula discussion in subsequent sessions.

## Key Claims

- A call option's payoff diagram is kinked at the strike price, making it asymmetric—unlike the symmetric payoff of a stock or futures contract
- Options are not "free lunch" instruments; even though call options have no downside in payoff at maturity, they have a purchase price that must be paid upfront
- A put option becomes valuable when the underlying stock price falls below the strike price, as it grants the right to sell at the higher strike price
- Option prices, particularly put options on the S&P 500, can reveal where the market expects volatility to go over coming months
- T-bill prices during the 2008 crisis reflected extreme demand for cash and safety, while option prices provided forward-looking market sentiment information
