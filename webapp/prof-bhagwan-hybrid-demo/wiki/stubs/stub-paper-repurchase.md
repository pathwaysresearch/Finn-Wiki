# Paper Repurchase

**Type**: RAG stub — full content in vector index, not in wiki

## Abstract

This paper by Bhagwan Chowdhry and Vikram Nanda proposes a dynamic model of corporate payout policies explaining why firms pay dividends despite their tax disadvantage relative to stock repurchases. The central argument is that because tender offer repurchases are associated with large stock-price increases (repurchase premia), they become expensive for firms unless the stock is sufficiently undervalued, making dividends the preferred distribution method in most periods. The model shows that firms accumulate cash and distribute it via dividends when repurchases are too costly, but use all accumulated cash for repurchases when the stock is sufficiently undervalued. The framework generates predictions about dividend smoothing, the relationship between dividends and earnings, repurchase frequency, and stock-price reactions to repurchase announcements.

## Key Claims

- Repurchase premia (the price increase firms must pay to acquire their own shares) make stock repurchases unattractive as a regular means of distributing cash, despite their tax advantage
- Firms distribute cash as dividends when their stock is not sufficiently undervalued by the market, carrying remaining cash forward for potential future repurchases
- When a firm's stock is sufficiently undervalued relative to managers' private information about true value, the firm distributes all accumulated cash through stock repurchases — analogous to the Myers and Majluf (1984) logic applied in reverse
- Dividends are smoothed over time and are positively related to both earnings innovations and previous-period dividends, consistent with Lintner (1956)
- Firms that repurchase less frequently are predicted to have higher dividend yields and larger stock-price reactions to repurchase announcements of a given size
- The stock-price reaction to a repurchase announcement of a given size is predicted to be increasing in the size of the previous period's dividend
