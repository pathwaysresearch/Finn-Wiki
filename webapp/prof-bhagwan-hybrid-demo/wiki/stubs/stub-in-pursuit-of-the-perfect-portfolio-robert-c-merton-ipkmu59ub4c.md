## Abstract

This is a video interview of Nobel laureate Robert C. Merton conducted by Andrew Lo at MIT, part of the "Perfect Portfolio" project accompanying the forthcoming book by Stephen Foerster and Andrew W. Lo. Merton discusses his personal journey from an early fascination with financial markets (buying his first stock at age 10-11) through his unconventional academic path into economics and finance, including his pivotal collaborations with Paul Samuelson on intertemporal optimization, warrants, and decision-making under uncertainty. The interview provides a firsthand account of the intellectual development of one of modern finance's most influential figures and the origins of key contributions to financial theory.

## Key Claims

- Merton bought his first stock (General Motors) around age 10-11 in the mid-1950s and frequented brokerage houses as a young person, developing early practical knowledge of financial markets and taxes
- Walter Heller's announcement that macroeconomic problems had been largely solved influenced Merton's decision to shift into economics, which was considered a "crazy change of fields" at the time
- Merton learned economics formally "backwards," starting with Paul Samuelson's *Foundations* book and working through intertemporal optimization and growth theory, where his mathematical background gave him a natural advantage
- Paul Samuelson engaged Merton to review his work on Hamiltonian mathematics applied to growth theory, and later invited him to co-author a paper on warrants — elevating him from research assistant to co-author
- Merton had no formal training in decision-making under uncertainty and learned expected utility theory "on the job" while combining it with his knowledge of intertemporal optimization under certainty from growth theory
