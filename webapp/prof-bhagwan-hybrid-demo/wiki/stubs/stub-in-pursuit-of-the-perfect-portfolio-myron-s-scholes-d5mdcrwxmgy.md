# In Pursuit Of The Perfect Portfolio: Myron S. Scholes

**Type**: RAG stub — full content in vector index, not in wiki

## Abstract

This is a video interview with Nobel laureate Myron Scholes, conducted by Steve Foerster as part of the "In Pursuit of the Perfect Portfolio" project (accompanying the forthcoming book by Foerster and Andrew Lo). Scholes discusses his views on portfolio construction, the Black-Scholes option pricing model and its technology, the importance of time diversification versus cross-sectional diversification, and why average return is a flawed measure for evaluating investments. The conversation also addresses the broader role of derivatives in financial markets, including the debate over whether they are "weapons of mass destruction."

## Key Claims

- Average return is a flawed measure for evaluating portfolio performance because investors only have one run of time — analogous to crossing a river where you need to know the depth at your specific crossing point, not the average depth
- Time diversification deserves more attention than cross-sectional diversification; allowing risk to fluctuate around a target introduces excess idiosyncratic volatility over time that can be mathematically shown to reduce portfolio performance
- The Black-Scholes technology involves creating a replicating portfolio using a combination of stocks and bonds that replicates option returns, with the ability to handle changing risk, volatility, and interest rates over time
- The derivation of the Black-Scholes model was a collaborative intellectual effort involving Fischer Black, Scholes, and Bob Merton, with distinct but mutually respected approaches leading to the differential equation
- Option prices provide valuable empirical information about risks in the marketplace, and the development of derivative technology has broad implications for investing
