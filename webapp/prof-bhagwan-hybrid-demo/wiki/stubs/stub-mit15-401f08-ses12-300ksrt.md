## Abstract

This is a lecture transcript from MIT's 15.401 Finance course (Fall 2008, Session 12) focused on option pricing theory. The professor introduces a simple but powerful binomial model for deriving a theoretical pricing formula for options and derivative securities. The lecture builds from a one-period binomial tree framework—where a stock can move up or down by factors u and d—to derive option prices, with a surprising result that one seemingly necessary parameter drops out of the pricing formula. The session continues from a prior lecture on the history of option pricing and MIT's special role in that history.

## Key Claims

- A stock price that can take on only two values tomorrow (up by factor u, down by factor d) implies the option can also take on only two values tomorrow
- The call option payoff is defined as the maximum of (stock price minus strike price) or zero: C1 = max(S1 - K, 0)
- The binomial model, despite being a strong simplification, can be extended to much greater complexity once the basic version is understood
- The probability of the stock going up (p) or down (1 - p) does not have to be 50/50
- One of the seemingly necessary parameters (including stock price, strike price, u, d, and probability) will surprisingly not appear in the final option pricing formula, which is described as a major source of insight
