## Abstract

This is a transcript of Session 7 from MIT 15.401 (Finance Theory), Fall 2008, taught by Professor Andrew Lo. The lecture covers real-time analysis of the yield curve, treasury bill yields, inflation expectations reflected in long-term bond yields, and extensive discussion of the 2008 financial crisis including the government rescue package, liquidity provision, and financial industry consolidation (e.g., Citigroup acquiring Wachovia, Bank of America acquiring Merrill Lynch). The session is notable for its contemporaneous commentary on market events during the height of the financial crisis and its implications for students' careers in finance.

## Key Claims

- The short end of the yield curve moved from 30-40 basis points to 71-72 basis points, with an intraday spike above 1%, reflecting rapid changes in market sentiment and demand for short-term treasuries
- The 30-year treasury yield at 422 basis points suggested slightly easing inflation concerns compared to prior weeks, though uncertainty remained
- Market prices at any point in time reflect current expectations and current information, serving as a window on market sentiment
- The government rescue package during the 2008 crisis was analogized to emergency room triage — stopping the bleeding first before addressing systemic structural problems like regulatory and behavioral failures
- Financial crises create opportunities: industry consolidation (e.g., Citigroup-Wachovia, Bank of America-Merrill Lynch) opens new competitive dynamics, and first-year MBA students were well-positioned to wait out the downturn
