# Paper Mmtrading

**Type**: RAG stub — full content in vector index, not in wiki

## Abstract

This paper by Bhagwan Chowdhry and Vikram Nanda analyzes what happens when a security trades at multiple locations simultaneously, examining how informed traders exploit private information across markets and how liquidity traders split orders to minimize costs. The model shows that the correlation between trading volume across markets depends on the proportion of "large" liquidity traders who can split trades, and that one market emerges as the dominant trading location. The paper also explores how timely release of price information by market makers at one location affects trading across markets, with implications for market structure regulation including the Securities Acts Amendments of 1975 and the push toward a nationwide competitive securities market.

## Key Claims

- When a security trades at multiple locations, informed traders' order sizes are perfectly correlated across markets in equilibrium, as are those of large liquidity traders
- The greater the proportion of liquidity trading by "large" traders who split trades across markets, the larger the correlation between volume in different markets and the smaller the informativeness of prices
- One market emerges as the dominant location for trading in a given security
- Cracking down on insider trading at one location does not necessarily drive trading away to other locations; that market may even attract the largest proportion of large liquidity and informed trading
- When informed traders can use their information for more than one trading period, the timely release of price information by market makers at one location adversely affects other markets
