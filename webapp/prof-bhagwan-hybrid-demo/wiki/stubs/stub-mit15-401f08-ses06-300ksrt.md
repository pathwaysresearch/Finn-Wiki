## Abstract

This is the transcript of Session 6 of MIT 15.401 (Finance Theory), Fall 2008, taught by Andrew Lo. The lecture covers arbitrage and the pricing of multiple fixed income securities, set against the backdrop of the unfolding 2008 financial crisis. Lo discusses real-time market events including Treasury yield curve movements, money market fund instability ("breaking the buck"), SEC short-selling bans on financial stocks, and the $700 billion government intervention — using these as concrete illustrations of fixed income pricing concepts. The session connects theoretical arbitrage principles to dramatic market dislocations happening during the semester.

## Key Claims

- Short-term Treasury bill yields dropped to as low as three basis points, indicating extraordinarily high prices for Treasury securities relative to historical standards
- The 30-year Treasury yield moved from 4% to 4.37% within a single week, reflecting market incorporation of inflation concerns tied to the $700 billion government bailout
- Money market funds "breaking the buck" — returning less than $1 per dollar invested — triggered massive redemptions and systemic confidence loss, as investors treated these funds like checking accounts
- The SEC mandated a temporary ban on short selling financial stocks to halt runs on those securities
- Rapid yield curve shifts raised the fundamental question of whether current market prices or prior week's prices were "correct," illustrating the complexity of price discovery during crises
