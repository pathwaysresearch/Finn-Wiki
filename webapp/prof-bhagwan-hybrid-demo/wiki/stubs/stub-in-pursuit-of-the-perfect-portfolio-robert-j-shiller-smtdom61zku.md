## Abstract

This is a video interview conducted by Andrew Lo (MIT) with Nobel laureate Robert J. Shiller (Yale University) as part of the "In Pursuit of the Perfect Portfolio" series, accompanying the forthcoming book by Stephen Foerster and Andrew Lo. The conversation covers Shiller's intellectual journey, his contributions to behavioral finance and macroeconomics, the development of the CAPE ratio (cyclically adjusted price-earnings ratio), his identification of the tech and real estate bubbles, and his debates with efficient market proponents like Eugene Fama. The discussion highlights the tension between rational/quantitative approaches and psychological/behavioral approaches in economics and finance.

## Key Claims

- Shiller's interest in behavioral economics stems from a fascination with people and crowd behavior rather than purely rational models
- The CAPE (cyclically adjusted price-earnings) ratio, developed by Shiller and John Campbell, smooths earnings over ten years to reduce year-to-year volatility and serves as a valuation metric
- Shiller successfully identified both the tech bubble and the real estate bubble early on, drawing on both anecdotal observations (e.g., overhearing "stock market" in restaurant conversations) and quantitative analytics like the CAPE ratio
- Shiller published his real estate work in specialty journals like Real Estate Economics rather than top-tier journals, and his Case-Shiller indexes elevated the field's prominence
- Despite public perception of a sharp disagreement, Shiller and Eugene Fama found during Nobel week debates that they don't fundamentally disagree on many points, though they differ on the role of psychology versus rationality in markets
