# In Pursuit of the Perfect Portfolio: Harry M. Markowitz

**Type**: RAG stub — full content in vector index, not in wiki

## Abstract

This is a video interview with Harry M. Markowitz, the 1990 Nobel Prize in Economics winner and father of modern portfolio theory, conducted by Steve Foerster as part of the "In Pursuit of the Perfect Portfolio" project (accompanying a forthcoming book by Foerster and Andrew W. Lo of MIT). Markowitz discusses the origins of his dissertation work at the University of Chicago, the intellectual development of mean-variance portfolio optimization, the role of diversification and correlation in portfolio risk, and his views on what constitutes the "perfect portfolio." The interview provides first-hand historical insight into how modern portfolio theory transformed institutional investment management from the 1950s onward.

## Key Claims

- John Burr Williams argued that the value of a stock should be the present value of its future dividends, which Markowitz interpreted as the expected value of future dividends under uncertainty
- If you are only interested in the expected value of a stock, you must be only interested in the expected value of a portfolio — a key logical step toward mean-variance optimization
- Williams' claim that sufficient diversification yields the mean return is only true if risks are independent/uncorrelated; with correlated risks, portfolio variance approaches the average covariance, not zero (from Chapter 5 of Markowitz's 1959 book)
- Markowitz's 2017 view would not recommend a simple 50/50 portfolio to a 25-year-old, given the power of compounding in equities (e.g., large cap growing to thousands vs. government bonds yielding far less over time)
- Peter Bernstein noted that younger people don't appreciate how different institutional investing was before the 1950s, underscoring the transformative impact of Markowitz's portfolio theory on the investment management industry
