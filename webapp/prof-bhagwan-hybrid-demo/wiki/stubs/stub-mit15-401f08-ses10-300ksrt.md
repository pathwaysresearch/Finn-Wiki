## Abstract

This is a transcript of Session 10 from MIT's 15.401 Finance Theory course (Fall 2008), taught by Andrew Lo. The lecture continues the discussion of futures and forward contracts, covering how these instruments work mechanically, how daily settlement (marking to market) reduces credit risk, and introduces a specific method for pricing forward and futures contracts. The session uses concrete examples—including live cattle futures and oil contracts—to illustrate zero-NPV transactions, margin accounts, and the role of futures exchanges in facilitating liquidity and reducing counterparty risk.

## Key Claims

- Futures contracts are zero-NPV transactions at inception; the futures price adjusts to make it so, since neither party would willingly enter a negative-NPV position
- No cash changes hands at the time a futures contract is struck; the contract is "worthless" at initiation but gains or loses value after the initial date
- Daily settlement (marking to market) via margin accounts reduces credit risk by requiring gains and losses to be settled each day between counterparties
- A futures position can be exited at any time before expiration by executing an opposite transaction (buying if you sold, selling if you bought), which cancels out delivery obligations
- Futures exchanges serve as critical infrastructure by encouraging liquidity, facilitating trading, and mitigating the risk that a counterparty defaults on obligations
