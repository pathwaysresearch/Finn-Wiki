## Abstract

This is a lecture transcript from MIT 15.401 (Finance Theory I), Session 14, taught by Andrew Lo. The lecture continues a discussion of risk and return, focusing on how to compute the mean and variance of a portfolio of multiple securities rather than individual stocks. The central insight developed is that portfolio variance depends critically on the covariances (correlations) between securities, not just individual stock variances, and that covariances become increasingly dominant as the number of holdings grows. This session provides the mathematical foundations for portfolio diversification and the mean-standard deviation framework used in modern portfolio theory.

## Key Claims

- Investors are assumed to prefer higher expected return and dislike risk as measured by volatility (standard deviation), depicted on a mean-standard deviation graph
- The expected return of a portfolio is a simple weighted average of the expected returns of individual securities
- The variance of a portfolio is NOT a simple weighted average of individual variances — it involves all pairwise covariance terms
- Portfolio variance can be represented as an n-by-n table with n² elements (n diagonal variance terms and n²−n covariance terms), requiring summation of all entries
- Covariances between securities are far more important in determining portfolio risk than the individual variances of the stocks, especially as n grows
