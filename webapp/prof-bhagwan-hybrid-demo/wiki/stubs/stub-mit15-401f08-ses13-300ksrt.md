## Abstract

This is a lecture transcript from MIT's 15.401 Finance Theory course (Fall 2008, Session 13) covering the empirical properties of stocks and bonds and the concept of market efficiency. The lecture develops historical perspectives on specific asset classes, presents key empirical facts about the US stock market (using data from approximately 1946–2001), and explains why efficient markets exhibit random price behavior. The instructor emphasizes how attempting to exploit predictable patterns in financial markets causes those patterns to disappear, distinguishing finance from deterministic sciences like physics.

## Key Claims

- Real interest rates have been slightly positive on average over the course of US financial history, but not by much
- In a well-functioning market, prices should react quickly to new information without delay
- Investors should not be able to earn abnormal returns after adjusting for risk in an efficient market
- If many participants try to predict and exploit price patterns, the patterns disappear, resulting in randomness — this is the basis of efficient market theory
- Low volatility alone does not indicate market efficiency, as illustrated by the early Shanghai Stock Exchange example with only two traded stocks
