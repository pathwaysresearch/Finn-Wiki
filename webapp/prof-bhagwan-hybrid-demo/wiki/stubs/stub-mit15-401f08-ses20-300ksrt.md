## Abstract

This is a transcript of MIT 15.401 (Finance Theory I) Session 20, taught by Andrew Lo in Fall 2008. The lecture continues the discussion of the Adaptive Markets Hypothesis (AMH) as an alternative to the Efficient Markets Hypothesis, exploring how markets evolve over time through biological and evolutionary mechanisms rather than remaining in a static equilibrium. Lo uses concrete examples from the hedge fund industry and stock market efficiency to illustrate how market dynamics, arbitrage opportunities, and investor behavior change with economic circumstances — particularly relevant given the financial crisis unfolding at the time of the lecture.

## Key Claims

- Markets are not always and everywhere efficient; instead they follow adaptive dynamics shaped by six properties introduced in the prior lecture
- Individuals act in their own self-interest but make mistakes; they learn, adapt, and competition drives adaptation and innovation through a process analogous to natural selection
- Market efficiency is not a universal constant like gravity — it changes over time and with circumstances, and the path of market dynamics shifts with major economic events
- Limited arbitrage and "free lunches" can exist from time to time, but consistent arbitrage opportunities (free lunch programs) are much harder to sustain as arbitrageurs erode them
- Major economic or traumatic events can permanently alter investor behavior and risk attitudes, as illustrated by the anecdote of a 95-year-old grandmother whose Depression-era experiences shaped her lifelong financial habits
