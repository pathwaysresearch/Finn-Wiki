## Abstract

This is a video interview conducted by Andrew Lo (MIT) with John C. "Jack" Bogle, founder of Vanguard and pioneer of index fund investing, as part of the "In Pursuit of the Perfect Portfolio" series accompanying the forthcoming book by Stephen Foerster and Andrew Lo. Bogle recounts the evolution of his thinking on mutual funds and indexation, tracing it from his Princeton thesis through the go-go era of the 1960s to the creation of the index fund. The conversation explores the history of the mutual fund industry, the case for passive investing, and the challenges posed by speculative fund management practices.

## Key Claims

- Bogle's Princeton thesis was one of the earliest academic examinations of the mutual fund industry, conducted with very limited data available at the time
- Mutual funds historically could make no claim to superiority over market indices, a finding that seeded Bogle's thinking about index funds
- The "go-go era" of the 1960s corrupted the fund industry, with go-go funds buying junk and letter stock at deep discounts to inflate portfolio valuations
- Before the go-go era, the mutual fund industry was conservative and slow-moving, and investors were generally not hurt by investing in funds
- The S&P 500 (originally the Standard & Poor's 50) was not widely followed early on; the Dow Jones dominated public attention before market-cap-weighted indexing gained prominence
