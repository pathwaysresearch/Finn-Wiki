## Abstract

This is the transcript of Session 9 from MIT 15.401 (Finance Theory I), Fall 2008, taught by Professor Andrew Lo. The lecture focuses on the definition, mechanics, and valuation of forward and futures contracts, with particular attention to the mark-to-market mechanism that distinguishes futures from forwards. Delivered during the 2008 financial crisis, the lecture also includes real-time commentary on market conditions, Federal Reserve interventions, liquidity concerns, and the opportunities that arise from financial crises. The session uses net present value formulas to value these derivative contracts and discusses practical applications such as hedging currency risk for multinational corporations.

## Key Claims

- The innovation of futures contracts over forward contracts is specifically the mark-to-market mechanism
- Valuation of forward and futures contracts relies on net present value formulas
- During financial crises, significant investment opportunities emerge for those who survive and can capitalize on distressed assets
- Exchange rate volatility poses severe challenges for manufacturing companies (illustrated with Caterpillar's sales data from 1980-1989), motivating the need for hedging instruments
- Technology can transform relatively backward, paper-and-pencil markets by providing better pricing information and transparency
