## Abstract

This is a transcript from MIT 15.401 (Finance Theory I), Session 15, taught by Andrew Lo. The lecture focuses on portfolio theory, specifically the construction of the efficient frontier using mean-variance optimization and the implications of combining imperfectly correlated securities. Lo demonstrates how diversification shifts the opportunity set to the northwest (higher return, lower risk) and contrasts this systematic portfolio approach with concentrated stock-picking strategies like Warren Buffett's, emphasizing that the analysis makes no assumptions about market mispricing or disequilibrium. The session builds toward foundational results linking risk and expected return.

## Key Claims

- Combining securities that are not perfectly correlated produces a bullet-shaped curve in expected return vs. volatility space, representing the portfolio opportunity set
- Investors should generally never hold 100% of a single stock because those individual positions lie inside the efficient frontier, not on it
- Adding more stocks to the opportunity set shifts the upper branch (efficient frontier) to the northwest, offering better risk-return trade-offs
- The analysis uses historical estimates of means, standard deviations, and the covariance matrix as inputs, though these parameters may change over time in reality
- Warren Buffett's approach fundamentally differs from mean-variance portfolio theory in that Buffett argues markets are mispriced (not in equilibrium), while the portfolio framework presented makes no such claims about whether prices are good or bad
