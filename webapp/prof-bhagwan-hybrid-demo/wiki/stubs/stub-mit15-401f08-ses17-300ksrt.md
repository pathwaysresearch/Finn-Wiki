## Abstract

This is a lecture transcript from MIT 15.401 (Finance Theory I), Session 17, taught by Andrew Lo. The lecture continues and concludes the discussion of the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM), examining its empirical performance through examples like Biogen's beta regression and the small-stock/large-stock size anomaly. The session then transitions into applications of the CAPM, particularly focusing on capital budgeting as the final major topic of the course. A central theme is distinguishing between beta (systematic, non-diversifiable risk) and sigma (total volatility) as the appropriate measure of risk that markets compensate.

## Key Claims

- The CAPM is an approximation to a more complex reality; individual stock regressions (e.g., Biogen with beta 1.42 and R-squared ~33%) show that the linear relationship with the market is imperfect and stocks carry significant idiosyncratic risk
- For size-sorted portfolios, the CAPM relationship holds reasonably well: small stocks (beta ~1.4, avg monthly return ~1.33%) have higher returns than large stocks (beta ~0.94, avg monthly return ~0.9%)
- For beta-sorted portfolios, the realized relationship between beta and expected return appears linear but the slope differs slightly from what the CAPM would predict based on the market risk premium
- Volatility (sigma) is not the correct measure of risk from the capital markets perspective; investors are compensated for bearing systematic risk (beta), not total volatility
- Higher volatility does not necessarily mean higher expected return; only non-diversifiable risk (beta) is rewarded by the market
