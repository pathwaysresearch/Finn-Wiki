# Mit15 401F08 Ses02 300K.Srt

**Type**: RAG stub — full content in vector index, not in wiki

## Abstract

This is the transcript of Session 2 of MIT 15.401 (Finance Theory I), taught by Professor Andrew Lo in Fall 2008. The lecture opens with a discussion of a classroom auction experiment from the previous session (involving an iPod and a hedge fund book) and then pivots to an extended real-time discussion of the 2008 financial crisis, specifically the government takeover of Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac. Lo uses the auction experiment as an analogy for the collapse in value of uncertain financial instruments and frames the unfolding crisis as motivation for the analytical tools the course will develop over subsequent lectures.

## Key Claims

- The classroom auction demonstrated that items of uncertain value sell at a steep discount (roughly $0.30 on the dollar), directly analogous to what happened to Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac's paper
- The 2008 government takeover of Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac was an event on a scale not seen since the Great Depression
- The Treasury intervened preemptively because waiting for a full day of reckoning would have made it very difficult to put the pieces back together
- All paper issued by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, plus new paper for financing additional mortgages, would be backed by the full faith and credit of the US Government
- The tools developed over the next three to four lectures would allow students to analyze and understand the unfolding financial crisis in real time

Classroom auction experiment, price discovery under uncertainty, 2008 financial crisis, Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac government takeover, US Treasury intervention, government-backed debt, discount on uncertain assets, moral hazard in government guarantees, Great Depression parallels, hedge funds, full faith and credit of the US Government, motivation for financial theory tools
