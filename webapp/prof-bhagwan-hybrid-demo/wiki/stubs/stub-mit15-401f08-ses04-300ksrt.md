## Abstract

This is a transcript from Session 4 of MIT's 15.401 Finance Theory course (Fall 2008), taught by Professor Andrew Lo. The lecture focuses on leverage, the subprime mortgage crisis, and the collapse of Lehman Brothers, using real-world examples such as home mortgage down payments and Lehman's financial statements to illustrate how leverage amplifies both gains and losses. The session connects fundamental finance concepts like mark-to-market accounting and risk to the unfolding 2008 financial crisis, making it a timely and pedagogically rich exploration of systemic financial risk.

## Key Claims

- Lehman Brothers had net revenues of $19 billion at the end of 2007, demonstrating it was a massive financial institution before its collapse
- A standard 20% down payment on a $500,000 home creates 5:1 leverage; a 10% decline in housing prices results in a 50% loss on invested capital
- With only 5% down ($25,000 on a $500,000 home), a 10% housing price decline results in a loss exceeding 100% of invested capital (net return of minus 200%)
- The perceived low risk of high leverage in housing was supported by the historical pattern of housing prices consistently going up in a smooth, orderly fashion (5%–10% annually)
- Subprime mortgages enabled millions of people to become homeowners who otherwise could not have, but the system became fragile when housing prices declined and borrowers could not afford mortgage payments
