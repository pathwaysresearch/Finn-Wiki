# Paper Bhag Submission

**Type**: RAG stub — full content in vector index, not in wiki

## Abstract

This paper by Bhagwan Chowdhry presents a formal theory of how a principal can overcome the public-goods failure in knowledge sharing by designing a "mission statement" consisting of a precise goal, a reporting protocol, and a prize. The model demonstrates that agents under-share knowledge in equilibrium, but the mechanism can raise effort, sustain full disclosure, and select a high-effort equilibrium when actions are strategic complements. A central result is the *Goldilocks theorem*: the optimal goal is set strictly above expected output at precisely the point where the marginal probability of success is maximized—too easy destroys incentives, too hard induces defection. The paper also shows that interdisciplinary missions generate supermodular gains, explaining the value of grand collective challenges.

## Key Claims

- In the absence of the mechanism, the unique equilibrium features zero disclosure even when full sharing would benefit every agent—a stark public goods failure
- The optimal mission prize raises effort above the private optimum by a margin proportional to the probability of tipping mission success at the margin (Goldilocks condition)
- A large prize does "double duty": it motivates effort directly and also provides indirect disclosure incentives through the knowledge spillover channel, reducing the need for separate monitoring-based disclosure systems
- Under ambitious goals (goal above expected output), efforts become strategic complements and the mission statement functions as a Schelling-style coordination device selecting the high-effort equilibrium; under easy goals, efforts become strategic substitutes encouraging free-riding
- Mission output is superlinear in combined effective knowledge (β > 1), capturing complementarity between disciplines, with connections to absorptive capacity (Cohen and Levinthal, 1990) and Jacobs (1970) on cross-disciplinary spillovers

## Topics Covered

Mission statements as mechanism design, knowledge sharing and public goods failures, Goldilocks theorem for optimal goal-setting, strategic complements vs. substitutes in effort, knowledge spillovers and absorptive capacity, prize design and disclosure incentives, supermodular gains from interdisciplinary collaboration, coordination games and focal points, complementarity of monetary and non-monetary instruments, N-agent extensions with multiplicative production functions, principal's optimal mechanism problem
