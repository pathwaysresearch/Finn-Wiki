## Abstract

This is the transcript of Session 5 of MIT 15.401 (Finance Theory), Fall 2008, taught by Professor Andrew Lo. The lecture covers the pricing of fixed income securities, particularly pure discount bonds and the term structure of interest rates, while simultaneously providing real-time commentary on the unfolding 2008 financial crisis — specifically the Lehman Brothers bankruptcy, the Barclays acquisition of Lehman's US operations, and the Federal Reserve's $85 billion loan to AIG. The session is a remarkable intersection of finance theory and a live financial crisis, making it valuable both as a pedagogical resource on bond pricing and as a primary account of expert analysis during the 2008 meltdown.

## Key Claims

- The Fed cut interest rates as the market had anticipated, with Fed funds futures pricing in at least a 25 basis point cut and a reasonable probability of a 50 basis point cut
- Barclays cherry-picked certain US operations of Lehman Brothers rather than acquiring the whole firm, partly because they could not get shareholder approval quickly enough and the Fed would not backstop hidden losses
- Retaining talent at acquired Lehman units significantly increased the cost of acquisition, as employees could walk and needed bonuses to stay
- The Fed's $85 billion loan to AIG was justified by AIG's systemic importance as an insurer in credit markets, making it far more critical to rescue than Lehman Brothers
- The price of a pure discount bond equals the face value discounted back using the relevant interest rate, and interest rates differ depending on the time horizon (term structure of interest rates)
