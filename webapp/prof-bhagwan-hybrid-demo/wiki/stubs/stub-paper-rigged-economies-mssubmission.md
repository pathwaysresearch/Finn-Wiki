# Paper Rigged Economies Mssubmission

**Type**: RAG stub — full content in vector index, not in wiki

## Abstract

This paper by Franklin Allen, Archishman Chakraborty, Bhagwan Chowdhry, and Nishant Ravi (December 2025) studies optimal executive compensation when shareholders observe neither the manager's feasible action set (hidden information) nor the chosen action (hidden action). The model shows that these informational frictions induce regime shifts in compensation within a competitive matching market of firms and managers, generating discontinuous pay hikes, abrupt changes in firm volatility, and weak links between pay and performance. The authors demonstrate that compensation outcomes that appear "rigged" — such as asymmetric pay responses to good and bad luck — can arise even with value-maximizing shareholders, providing an alternative to the rent extraction/managerial entrenchment view of executive pay.

## Key Claims

- Hidden information (shareholders cannot observe the firm's opportunity set) combined with hidden action (shareholders cannot monitor CEO decisions) generates regime shifts in executive compensation
- Competitive matching between firms and managers explains the post-1980 regime shift where CEO earnings increased dramatically alongside increased firm volatility
- Optimal CEO pay can be high even when the stock price is low, consistent with empirical evidence
- The least talented manager's compensation can exceed outside options even when potential managers are abundant, driven by competitive equilibrium pressures
- Asymmetric CEO pay responses to good versus bad luck (documented by Garvey and Milbourn, 2006) can emerge from value-maximizing behavior rather than requiring managerial capture of corporate boards
- Slight imbalances in the total supply of managers versus firms can select between firm-optimal and manager-optimal stable matchings, resulting in large differences in managerial pay, firm volatility, and total surplus
