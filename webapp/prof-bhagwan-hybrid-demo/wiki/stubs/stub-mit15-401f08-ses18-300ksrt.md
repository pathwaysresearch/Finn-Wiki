## Abstract

This is a lecture transcript from MIT 15.401 (Finance Theory I), Session 18, taught by Professor Andrew Lo. The lecture focuses on alternatives to the Net Present Value (NPV) rule used in capital budgeting and project financing, including the payback period method. Lo argues firmly that NPV is the superior method but explains why practitioners still use other approaches, covering their advantages, disadvantages, and practical motivations such as career risk and liquidity concerns. The session continues from a prior lecture on NPV applications to capital budgeting.

## Key Claims

- NPV is the recommended and superior method for capital budgeting decisions compared to its alternatives
- The payback period method is flawed because it adds cash flows from different time periods without discounting, which is analogous to adding different currencies together
- Managers may favor short payback period projects due to career risk rather than maximizing shareholder value, which is a misalignment of incentives
- If compensation contracts properly incentivize managers (e.g., through stock ownership), they should focus on maximizing NPV rather than payback period
- Liquidity concerns sometimes cited as a justification for payback period should instead be explicitly factored into NPV analysis through the term structure of borrowing costs
