## Abstract

This is a lecture transcript from MIT 15.401 (Finance Theory I), Fall 2008, Session 19, taught by Andrew Lo. The lecture focuses on the Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) and its primary challenger, behavioral finance. Lo explores cognitive biases that undermine the assumption of rational market participants—such as loss aversion, overconfidence, and framing effects—using interactive classroom experiments to demonstrate how real decision-making deviates from theoretical predictions. The lecture also references real-world events, including stock price reactions of companies involved in the Challenger space shuttle disaster (e.g., Morton Thiokol), as case studies for market efficiency.

## Key Claims

- The Efficient Market Hypothesis implies that profitable opportunities cannot persist because competitive markets would have already exploited them—analogized via the "$100 bill on the sidewalk" economist joke
- Behavioral finance challenges market efficiency by arguing that market participants are irrational and suffer from systematic biases including loss aversion, anchoring, framing, overconfidence, overreaction, herding, and mental accounting
- Classroom choice experiments demonstrate that students' most popular combined choices (A and D) are strictly dominated by the less popular combination (B and C), which is equivalent to the popular choice plus $10,000 in cash—illustrating irrational decision-making
- People exhibit loss aversion: they are willing to take more risk when facing losses than when facing gains, an asymmetry inconsistent with standard utility maximization and efficient market theory
- Stock price movements of companies involved in the space shuttle program (including Morton Thiokol) provide an empirical lens for examining how quickly and accurately markets incorporate information
