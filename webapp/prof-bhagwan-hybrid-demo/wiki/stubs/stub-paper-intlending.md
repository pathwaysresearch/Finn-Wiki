# Paper Intlending

**Type**: RAG stub — full content in vector index, not in wiki

## Abstract

This paper by Bhagwan Chowdhry (UCLA) addresses the fundamental question of how enforceability is achieved in international debt contracts, given the absence of conventional legal enforcement mechanisms against sovereign borrowers. The model shows that banks deny credit to defaulting borrowers to maintain a reputation as "tough" lenders, thereby disciplining their other borrowers, and that syndicated lending structures make the penalty for default severe enough to deter voluntary default. Key predictions include that interest rates charged on loans are lower for larger borrowers and that, for any given borrower, interest rates may fall after successive defaults. The paper is set against the backdrop of the rapid growth in commercial bank lending to developing countries and the concentration of borrowing among larger sovereign borrowers.

## Key Claims

- Each bank announces a policy of denying credit to defaulters and adheres to it to maintain its reputation of being a tough bank, which disciplines its other borrowers
- Loans are made by syndicates of banks to make the penalty for default severe enough that borrowers choose not to default voluntarily
- The interest rate charged on international loans is smaller for larger borrowers
- For any given borrower, the interest rate may fall after each successive default
- Banks find it costly to deny credit to defaulters (forgoing future profits from fixed-cost investments in borrower information), but do so to prevent contagion of default among other borrowers
- Mechanisms such as debt-equity swaps, exit bonds, and rescheduling at below-market rates allow borrowers to discharge debt overhang and seek fresh financing, all involving partial default
- Many small and poor less-developed countries cannot access private capital markets at all, partly due to fixed costs in information acquisition
- Contractual seniority provisions (as suggested by Stiglitz and Weiss) may not effectively restrain fresh lending to defaulting sovereign borrowers, as courts may allow priority for new debt
