# Stock Options

Stock options are contracts that give the holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy (call) or sell (put) a company's stock at a predetermined price (the strike price) before or at expiration. They serve two primary functions in finance:

1. **As traded derivatives**: In public markets, stock options are used for hedging risk (e.g., protective puts) and speculation on price movements. Their pricing and behavior are explained by models such as the Black‑Scholes framework, which links option values to underlying stock price, volatility, time to expiration, and interest rates.

2. **As executive compensation**: Firms grant stock options to align managerial incentives with shareholder value. By linking pay to stock performance, options motivate executives to pursue strategies that increase firm value. Empirical work shows that this incentive structure often coincides with higher operating risk, producing a **pay‑volatility co‑movement**—a pattern where generous equity‑linked pay and elevated firm volatility arise together as firms shift to growth‑oriented, risk‑taking regimes.

Thus, stock options bridge derivative markets and corporate governance: they are financial instruments whose market use informs pricing theory, and whose use in compensation shapes real‑world risk‑taking and pay dynamics.
