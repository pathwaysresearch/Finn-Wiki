# Stock Options Dual Roles: Information and Incentives

Stock options are commonly viewed simply as a form of equity-based compensation for executives. However, they simultaneously serve two distinct economic functions. First, as traded derivatives, options contain valuable information about the full distribution of future returns, especially tail‑risk outcomes. Market prices of options reflect investors’ beliefs about extreme events, making them a window into systemic risk. Second, when granted to top managers, options act as high‑powered incentives that encourage risk‑taking and the pursuit of aggressive growth strategies. This duality creates a tension: the same contract that signals market‑wide risk concerns can also induce the very risk‑taking those signals are meant to monitor. Consequently, relying on option grants as the primary tool for aligning executive pay with performance may obscure the informational content that options provide to outside observers, while using option prices to gauge risk may be confounded by the incentive effects those same contracts create for insiders.
