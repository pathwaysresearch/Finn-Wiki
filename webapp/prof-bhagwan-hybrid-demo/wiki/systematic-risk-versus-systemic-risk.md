# Systematic Risk Versus Systemic Risk

These two terms are frequently confused but refer to distinct concepts in finance.

## Systematic Risk

Systematic risk — also called **market risk** or **undiversifiable risk** — is risk due to market-wide news that affects all stocks simultaneously. It cannot be eliminated through diversification. Because investors cannot eliminate systematic risk, they must be compensated for holding it via a **risk premium**.

The **Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)** quantifies this:

> rᵢ = r_f + βᵢ × (E[R_Mkt] − r_f)

Beta (β) measures a security's sensitivity to systematic shocks. Only systematic risk — not diversifiable (idiosyncratic) risk — affects the risk premium investors require.

## Systemic Risk

Systematic risk is distinct from **systemic risk**, which is the risk of collapse of an entire financial system due to the failure of a single entity or group of interconnected entities. The 2008–2009 financial crisis is the canonical example: the failure of large, interconnected financial institutions froze credit markets, wiped out savings, caused unemployment to soar, and led to over 100 bank failures or forced mergers.

Systemic risk is central to monetary policy (Federal Reserve), fiscal policy, and financial regulation — particularly regarding institutions considered **too large to fail**.

## Key Distinctions

| Dimension | Systematic Risk | Systemic Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Domain | Asset pricing | Macroeconomics & regulation |
| Cause | Economy-wide shocks | Failure of interconnected institutions |
| Diversifiable? | No | No — contained via regulation |
| Primary audience | Investors, portfolio managers | Central banks, regulators, governments |
