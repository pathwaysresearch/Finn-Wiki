# Why Invoicing in Dollars Is a Big Deal

Imagine you're a Brazilian coffee exporter pricing your bags at $100. Every time the dollar moves against the real, your actual income in reais changes — unpredictably, in either direction. You have to hedge that risk, which costs money, or just absorb the uncertainty. Your US competitor invoicing in dollars knows exactly what they'll receive. **No uncertainty, no hedging cost — just by virtue of their currency.**

Now multiply that across every supply chain and trade deal on the planet.

**Three reasons the dollar holds its position — though none are absolute:**

**1. Prices are sticky — for manufactured goods.**
Long-term contracts for machinery, electronics, and services don't reprice instantly. So when exchange rates move, the dollar price holds — meaning dollar movements affect everyone's costs simultaneously. The US controls a kind of global volume knob through its monetary policy.

**2. Coordination problem, not lock-in.**
Dollar hedging markets are deep precisely because everyone uses dollars — but that advantage is bounded. If it grew too large, collective switching to euros or yen would become worthwhile. The real barrier is that no individual actor gains by moving first. It's a stable equilibrium, not an unbreakable one — the euro has already made partial inroads in Europe's neighborhood, and sustained US fiscal or political mismanagement could accelerate a shift.

**3. Commodities use dollars as their benchmark.**
Oil, wheat, copper — global exchanges quote, settle, and clear in dollars. To participate in commodity markets, you need dollars, which pulls every commodity-dependent industry into the dollar system automatically.

## The US-Specific Superpower
The US borrows in its own currency, so it can never be crushed by a rising dollar the way other countries can. And in every global crisis, everyone scrambles *for* dollars — which makes the dollar stronger precisely when the US needs cheap borrowing most.

## Bottom Line
The dollar's dominance is a self-reinforcing equilibrium — real, but not inevitable. It persists because switching is collectively costly to coordinate, not because alternatives are fundamentally inferior.
