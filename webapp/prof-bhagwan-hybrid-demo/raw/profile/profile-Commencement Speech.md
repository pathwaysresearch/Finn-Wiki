# Rebel Against Risk
*On the Meaning of Life | Commencement Speech*
***
I am a finance professor. My job is to think about risk – how to measure it, price it, share it. Finance is not, traditionally speaking, a profession associated with deep existential insight. We are better known for causing financial crises than for solving the human condition.

And yet.

A few years ago I spent a day on a Buddhist retreat with Stephen Batchelor — the scholar who wrote Buddhism Without Beliefs, which is exactly what it sounds like: Buddhism for people who find the reincarnation parts a bit much. Somewhere in the afternoon, sitting on a cushion trying to think about nothing, something clicked. The first noble truth — existence is suffering – sounded exactly like something Darwin would have said after a bad week. Life is uncertain. We have less control than we think. The Buddha called it impermanence. I call it exogenous risk. Same thing. The Buddha just had better branding.

That was the beginning of an idea I have been developing ever since: life is a risk management problem. Once you see it this way, the Buddha, Camus, the Bhagavad Gita, and the logic of financial markets all start pointing at the same answer. Which is either a profound insight or a sign that I need to find a therapist.

**First: The Diagnosis. And It Is Not Good News.**

Here is something remarkable. Five great traditions – Buddhism, existentialism, Joyce, Kundera, and the Bhagavad Gita — agree on almost nothing. Different centuries, different continents, different everything. And yet they all agree on one thing: there is no ground.

Buddhism calls it Sunyata – Emptiness. Nothing has a fixed essence. The self you are so carefully protecting and curating for Instagram? Also empty. Also contingent. No permanent core.

Camus called it the Absurd – the collision between our desperate need for meaning and the universe's complete indifference. Traditional sources of meaning – religion, progress, human nature itself — turn out to be things we made up. The universe did not hand them to us. The universe, frankly, has not been returning our calls.

Kundera called it lightness. If nothing repeats, nothing carries ultimate weight. History is a series of accidents. Even suffering has no inherent meaning. This is why The Unbearable Lightness of Being is unbearable — not because it is pessimistic, but because it is accurate.

[Page 2]
And then the Gita. Arjuna on the battlefield, moments before the fighting starts, suddenly
feels that all action is meaningless, that duty is arbitrary, that dharma itself seems
questionable. He basically has a full existential crisis while sitting in a chariot. His
charioteer – who turns out to be Krishna, Bhagwan himself – then has to give him a two-
hour pep talk to get him moving again. This is the Gita. It is not a TED talk.

Four traditions, centuries apart, same conclusion: the ground is not solid. What
philosophers call groundlessness, I call risk. And risk I know how to think about.

**Second: Why We Get It Wrong – And Why That Is Darwin's Fault.**

Start with Darwin. I always start with Darwin. It saves time.

The first-order problem of life is survival and procreation. Everything else — art,
philosophy, civilization, this speech – is what biologists call second-order. Commentary.
Expensive commentary, but commentary.

Now, survival requires coping with uncertainty. Here is the puzzle: we are spectacularly
bad at this. We systematically underestimate how much uncertainty there is. Why?
Because the illusion of control makes us happier. And action kept our ancestors alive. The
optimist hunted. The pessimist sat in the cave composing accurate but paralysing
assessments of the situation, and did not reproduce.

So we evolved to be overconfident. We are, as a species, constitutionally bad at reading
our own risk exposure. This is not a character flaw. It is a feature. A very inconvenient
feature, but a feature.

Crises fix this temporarily. When something terrible happens that has no obvious
connection to anything you chose, you are briefly, painfully recalibrated. Traleg Rinpoche
– a Tibetan Buddhist teacher – said something I find both alarming and liberating: in
depression, you find intelligence and accuracy. Psychology literature agrees. Realistic
people are mildly depressed.

I want to be clear: I am not recommending depression as a lifestyle. I am saying that the
traditions which tell us to face reality as it is — uncertain, volatile, unguaranteed — are
onto something. You cannot build a workable life on a false picture of the world. You can
try. It just does not work very well.

**Third: What Actually Helps. Finally, Some Good News.**

Given a volatile world, we have two strategies. The first is self-insurance.

Self-insurance means reducing the variance of your own outcomes through your own
choices. The Buddha's Eightfold Path, which sounds mystical, is — when you look closely
— almost entirely about this. Choose a life with smaller variance. Do not lie, steal, or

[Page 3]
commit sexual misconduct. Not because the universe will punish you. Because eventually
you will get caught, and the downside is enormous. Look at Enron. Lying worked
brilliantly right up until it did not.

Morality, in this framing, is a variance-reduction technology. I realize this is the least
romantic description of ethics ever formulated. But it explains why ethical behavior is not
just socially nice but functionally necessary. The cheater gets ahead in the short run. In
the long run, the cheater is Enron.

Moral consistency also makes you trustworthy. When you live predictably and
transparently, people can rely on you. When they can rely on you, they help you when
things go wrong. Personal ethics and social mutual aid are the same mechanism at
different scales. One Buddha, two birds.

But self-insurance has hard limits. You can diversify your portfolio. You should absolutely
diversify your portfolio. Buy index funds. Do not pick individual stocks. I have a PhD in
finance and I am telling you: do not pick individual stocks. Nobody consistently beats the
market. Nobody.

You cannot, however, index-fund your way out of illness, grief, or the death of someone
you love. At some point, the only hedge available is other people.

This is where risk-sharing enters. And this – I am now convinced — is the deepest answer
to the meaning of life. Markets are formal risk-sharing mechanisms insurance,
securities - just promises to move resources from good times to bad times. Social bonds
are informal ones: friendship, community, the unspoken deal that when things fall apart
for you, I will show up. Finance is a formalization of this impulse. The impulse is older
than civilization. It might just be what civilization is for.

# Fourth: Why Generosity Feels Good – Which Should Make Us Suspicious.

Here is a question I have been bothering people with for twenty years: why did evolution
program us to feel good when we give?

The standard answer is reciprocal altruism – I scratch your back, I expect you to scratch
mine. This is part of the story but not all of it. Giving to a stranger you will never see again
– this also feels good. The reciprocity story cannot explain this. The reciprocity story is a
bit embarrassed by this.

My hypothesis: generosity resets the comparison benchmark. We are hedonic adaptation
machines. We normalize to our current level of wellbeing almost immediately and start
wanting more. Someone gets a raise. For two weeks, they are thrilled. Then it becomes
the new normal, and they want another raise. This is the human condition and, hey Ram,
it is exhausting.

[Page 4]
Generosity interrupts the cycle. When you give when you see others with less - the reference point shifts. What was sufficient starts to feel like abundance again. This is not self-congratulation. It is recalibration. Generosity is one of the few reliable hacks for the hedonic treadmill.

I have seen this in my work on financial inclusion in India. Communities that genuinely care about each other are more resilient to shocks not just emotionally, but economically. Real empathy generates real trust. Real trust generates real cooperation. You cannot fake your way to this. Evolution tried faking it for millions of years and gave up. The real thing works better.

And here is what Buddhism adds: if the self has no fixed permanent nature, then caring for others is not a sacrifice of self. It is an expansion of self. Generosity is not charity. It is enlightened self-interest, once you have correctly defined the self.

### Fifth: Camus, Kundera, and the Part Nobody Wants to Hear.

Albert Camus identified something the economic framing misses. I can tell you: share, cooperate, reduce variance, build trust. All correct. But this does not answer the prior question. What about the gap between our hunger for meaning and the silence that comes back? What about the boulder that will always – always – roll back down?

Camus says: push it anyway. The struggle itself is enough. One must imagine Sisyphus happy. His reasoning: if we are all condemned to the same absurd fate, that shared condition is the foundation for solidarity. We choose connection in full awareness that the universe would not notice if we chose otherwise.

I find this both bleak and oddly exhilarating. It is the philosophical equivalent of: well, we are all going down, so we might as well be kind to each other. Which turns out, upon reflection, to be a very good reason to be kind to each other.

But Kundera corrects something Camus is too heroic to admit. Knowing the boulder rolls back does not make it easier to watch. Intellectual acceptance and emotional habitability are not the same thing. Kundera's characters understand their situation perfectly. They are still destroyed by it. Tereza knows her jealousy has no cosmic foundation. She cannot stop it anyway. Philosophy does not fix this. Understanding is not peace.

The honest version of this worldview must acknowledge the psychological cost. Meditation helps — it trains the capacity to hold difficult truths without being demolished by them. But it is practice, not solution. The difficulty does not go away. You just get better, gradually, at sitting with it.

This is wisdom: gradual improvement in sitting with difficulty. Not transcendence. Gradual improvement. You're welcome.

# The Synthesis. Or: What the Buddha, Darwin, Camus, and Your Grandmother All Agree On.

Let me bring this together, because I think these traditions are complementary, not competing.

The economic framing provides the skeleton: life is a risk management problem. The most powerful strategy available — more powerful than diversification, more powerful than savings, more powerful than any financial instrument I know — is genuine commitment to other people. Self-insurance through ethical behavior. Risk-sharing through community. Trust through consistency.

The philosophical traditions add the flesh. Buddhism says the self you are protecting was never as solid as you thought — which is, once you adjust to it, actually liberating. Camus says the absence of cosmic guarantee is a reason for fiercer engagement, not retreat. The Gita says act without attachment to results — you cannot control outcomes, only inputs. Kundera says: do not pretend it is easy.

Different days call for different prescriptions. Some days you need Buddhist equanimity. Some days you need Camusian defiance. Some days you need the Gita's surrender. Some days you just need a stiff drink and Kundera. No tradition has the complete answer because the problem changes shape every day.

Altruism is good for you. This is not a paradox. It is the punchline.

I have been building toward this for a long time. Through papers, through retreats, through conferences where I went to meet people rather than listen to talks — which I recommend, by the way; the talks are almost never as good as the conversations in the corridor. The ideas have slowly sharpened into something I can now say plainly.

Once you know the ground is not there, you stop waiting for it to hold you up. You start holding each other instead.

**That is the meaning of life.**
