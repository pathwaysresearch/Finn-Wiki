Abstract
We study optimal compensation when shareholders observe neither the manager's feasible
action set nor his chosen action. Such hidden information induces regime shifts in compensation
within a competitive matching market of firms and managers. Because managerial talent is
portable, equilibrium wages feed back into firms' regime choices, generating discontinuous pay
hikes, abrupt changes in firm volatility, and weak links between pay and performance. Com-
pensation can rise above outside options for all managers, even when (potential) managers are
abundant. Thus, we explain several well-documented empirical regularities, using a competitive
model with a single within-match friction, and no rent seeking by managers.
JEL classification: D82, D86, G34.
Key Words: executive compensation, hidden information, hidden action, assortative match-
ing, regime shifts.

# 1 Introduction
CEO compensation is contentious, particularly in times of recessions, economic crises and increasing inequality. Piketty and Saez (2014) document that the share of income going to the top 10% has been increasing in the past few decades, reaching nearly 50% in the United States and 35% in Europe. They show that “a very large part of the rise in the top 10% income share comes from the top 1% (or even the top 0.1%). This is largely due to the rise of top executive compensation in large U.S. corporations (both financial and non-financial).”

The phenomenal rise in CEO compensation in the last few decades is also documented in Frydman (2019), Frydman and Jenter (2010), Kaplan and Rauh (2013), and Edmans, Gabaix and Jenter (2017). In 1980, CEO pay was on average 40 times the pay of the median worker, a ratio that had grown to over 300 by 2015 in the United States. Since 1980, CEO pay in S&P 500 companies has grown 600%.¹

The popular account of what has caused CEO pay to rise dramatically is the “rent extraction” view which argues that contracts are effectively set by executives themselves to maximize their own rents. In this view, the compensation process is rigged. On the contrary, the “shareholder value” view argues that compensation contracts are chosen to maximize value for shareholders, taking into account the competitive market for executives and the need to provide adequate incentives (Edmans, Gabaix and Jenter, 2017). This paper belongs to the second school. Yet, we show why competitive outcomes may look rigged. High equilibrium levels of managerial pay can lead to regime changes by value-maximizing firms that result in significantly higher levels of incentive pay but lower shareholder value.

We propose a model of competitive matching between firms and managers under within-firm agency conflicts. The within-firm frictions are of the hidden information and hidden action variety, and managerial skills are portable across firms.² Our results are consistent with three important empirical observations.

1. Before 1980, CEO compensation remained stable relative to several benchmarks but then there was a regime shift and CEO earnings increased dramatically, as did firm volatility.
2. Optimal CEO pay can be high even when the stock price is low.
3. The least talented manager’s compensation can be high, leading to widespread pay escalation due to competition, even with an excess supply of potential managers.

A number of academic scholars have argued that extremely high CEO pay could arise if managerial talent is scarce but portable across firms (Gabaix and Landier, 2008; Kaplan and Rauh, 2013, Edmans and Gabaix, 2016). When CEOs are incentivized to increase the value of the equity they hold—typically around 0.3-0.4% in large firms—this relatively small ownership stake can have a disproportionately large impact on firm value, particularly in very large firms that compete for a limited pool of highly talented CEOs. Edmans, Gabaix and Jenter (2017) show that the large increase in CEO pay from the 1980’s to early 2000’s mirrors the increase in size of the largest firms in the U.S. Although total pay has risen for firms of all sizes, the increases were steeper in larger firms. They caution, however, that “while the level of pay has generally increased over time, this trend has been neither constant nor uniform, contrary to popular belief.” Like Frydman and Saks (2010), they note that the CEO compensation levels from World War II to 1970 were remarkably flat, even though firms grew considerably during that time. Clearly, something fundamental changed in the 1980s. This is our first empirical observation above. We explore this key issue, while studying returns to managerial talent.

Edmans, Gabaix and Jenter (2017) also suggest that for “future research, it would be interesting to model the trade-off between inducing executives to take ‘good’ risks and discouraging them from taking ‘bad’ risks.” We take up exactly this suggestion. We show that regime changes in the risk profile of firms are jointly determined with widespread escalation in executive pay, which not only explains the timing of pay escalation after the 1980s but also generates several additional testable implications about the types of firms for which we are likely to see pay escalation.

We start by formalizing the trade-off between good and bad risks in an agency model with two key informational frictions. One, shareholders cannot monitor the CEO’s decisions (a hidden action problem), which matters because the CEO has personal preferences that are not perfectly aligned with those of the firm’s shareholders (because of a preference for power, media recognition, socially responsible projects, etc.). Two, shareholders cannot fully observe the set of opportunities actually available to the firm (a hidden information problem): the manager knows whether or not a valuable but risky growth opportunity is available, but shareholders do not.

To incentivize the CEO to undertake the valuable growth opportunity whenever available, an optimal contract must offer high-powered incentives. But such incentives have a perverse effect: when the growth opportunity is unavailable, the CEO may be tempted by the contract to undertake a different, value-destroying risky action that looks similar to a value-maximizing one (Brown, Harlow, and Starks, 1996). To prevent this, the contract must also handsomely compensate the CEO for choosing a safe, low-risk project whenever better options are not available. The hidden information problem thus creates a need to provide strong incentives in both states of the world—when a good growth opportunity is available and when it is not—driving the average level of compensation

[Page 4]
to be quite high, regardless of the likelihood of good growth opportunities. So, when the expected
benefit from pursuing growth is not large enough, it is optimal for the firm to forego growth and
pay the manager less.

The market may infer information about the decisions taken by the manager. When it learns
that value-increasing growth opportunities have likely been undertaken, the stock price will rise.
Otherwise, the stock price will fall. If the manager gets higher private benefits (or pays lower
private costs) from decisions that generate lower expected cash flows, managerial compensation
will be positively correlated with firm performance. But if the manager is partially aligned with
shareholders, with higher private benefits from taking decisions that yield riskier cash flows (good
or bad), managerial compensation will be negatively correlated with firm performance.

The contracting problem thus provides an explanation of the evidence that CEO pay is not
tightly related to firm performance, which is our second empirical observation above. Bertrand
and Mullainathan (2001) find that executives are paid for firm performance that might be driven
by good performance of the entire sector, largely unrelated to managerial actions. This result
may not be surprising in an efficient market in which investors properly anticipate the expected
effects of managerial actions (Holmstrom, 1979; Murphy, 2013). However, Garvey and Milbourn
(2006) document a suspicious asymmetry in CEO pay CEO pay is high following positive sector
performance ("good luck") but CEO pay does not appear to be sufficiently low following negative
sector performance (“bad luck"). This is one of the main pieces of evidence for the “rent extraction"
view that the compensation process is rigged. It has led to concerns that entrenched managers may
have captured corporate boards and manipulated the pay-setting process to their own advantage
(Bebchuk and Fried, 2003; see Kuhnen and Zwiebel, 2009 for a model of compensation under
entrenchment). We show that even value-maximizing shareholders may set optimal compensation
contracts that reward CEOs for luck, with high compensation accompanied by a low stock price.
Our model is therefore consistent with the second empirical observation above.

We next investigate how our contracting problem affects two-sided matching between managers
of heterogeneous talent with firms of varying quality. Talent and quality are complementary inputs
into the expected value of the risky but valuable growth opportunity. We first characterize the set
of stable matchings in balanced markets, where the two sides of the market are of the same total
size. We show that all managers rank stable matchings in the same way, as do firms. But the
two common rankings on the two sides of the market are exactly opposed. The manager-optimal
(stable) matching is the one that is the best for all managers, while the firm-optimal (stable)
matching is the one that is most preferred by all firms (i.e., shareholders). Demand for talent is
the highest possible in the former and the lowest possible in the latter.

An unbalanced market, where the two sides are not of equal size, has a unique stable matching.

[Page 5]
When there are more potential managers than firms, we look at the balanced market that is
created by keeping the excess mass of the least talented managers unmatched. We show that
the unique equilibrium is the firm-optimal matching of this balanced market, with wages driven
down by the mass of unmatched managers. However, under an aggregate scarcity of managers, the
unique equilibrium selects the manager-optimal stable matching of the balanced market created by
keeping the excess mass of the lowest quality firms unmatched. The two matching outcomes differ
significantly in the level and distribution of executive pay.

In the firm-optimal matching, only high-quality firms, for whom undertaking risky growth is
a dominant strategy, pursue such opportunities by attracting top managerial talent with high
incentive pay. Other lower-quality firms, for whom the probability of encountering the growth
opportunity is too remote, prefer to forego risky growth and hire less talented managers at (dis-
continuously) lower pay. In the manager-optimal matching, in contrast, all firms choose to pursue
risky growth opportunities, no matter how unlikely they are to materialize, forced entirely by the
equilibrium pressures of high managerial pay to do so.

If managers could collectively enforce a stable matching, they would pick the manager-optimal
one. But such managerial capture of the pay process is not necessary for high executive pay.
Competitive pressures generated by slight imbalances in the total supply of managers versus firms
are enough to select one or the other stable matching, resulting in large differences in managerial
pay, firm volatility, and total surplus. In fact, it is the firm-optimal matching that has a flavor of
rent extraction, since total surplus is (at least weakly) lower in each firm, and shareholders get a
larger share of this smaller pie. The manager-optimal matching maximizes the total pie.

These results provide one explanation for the historical evidence of a dramatic escalation in ex-
ecutive pay since the 1980s. Technological progress, productivity growth, financial market changes,
and regulatory reforms may have eased entry into the market for potential firms. Absent a corre-
sponding growth in the supply of potential managers, perhaps because of social constraints or the
high costs of education and training, one should expect a cascade of regime changes across firms
over time, and an attendant large escalation in executive pay.³ An improvement in the quality
distribution of firms, or a worsening of the talent distribution of managers, would further add to
the pay escalation while lowering payoffs on the firm side.

A scarcity in the total supply of potential managers is not necessary for widespread escalation
in executive pay. If sufficiently many (of even the less talented) managers have good enough out-
side options, there could be a *relative* scarcity of managers for lower quality firms that seek to

In addition to rationalizing compensation patterns over time, our framework generates a rich
set of sharp, testable cross-sectional predictions. It provides a causal mechanism for the empirically
documented correlation between CEO pay and volatility, predicting that high compensation and
high firm risk are jointly determined.⁴ More pointedly, our model suggests that large, discontinuous
jumps in CEO pay should be observed primarily in firms that undergo a “regime shift” from
safe, low-growth strategies to risky, high-growth ones. Hidden information frictions are key to
this insight. The shift to a high-pay/high-risk regime can optimally occur with no corresponding
increase in shareholder value, explaining the often-puzzling disconnect between pay hikes and firm
performance. The cascade effect described above provides a market-based explanation for the
widespread and rapid nature of pay increases post-1980s. It also implies that the compensation for
skilled CEOs was much more sensitive to their ability post-1980s, compared to the relatively flat
CEO compensation before the 1970s (Frydman and Saks, 2010).⁵

# 2 A Contracting Problem

A principal of a firm (the shareholders collectively) must design a compensation contract for an agent (the CEO or manager) at date 0. Because of informational frictions and agency conflicts that we detail below, the compensation contract must incentivize the manager to engage in shareholder value-maximizing activities. The manager's choices affect the distribution of future cash flows, and his compensation is contingent on these cash flows. All agents are risk-neutral, and the risk-free rate is normalized to zero.

After accepting his contract on date 0, the CEO faces a choice among (at most) three alternatives. He can take a "safe" decision, $d = S$, that generates cash flows of \$1 at date 1. He can also take a "bad" decision, $d = B$, that yields cash flows on date 2 equal to $R$ w.p. $p_B$ and $r$ w.p. $1 - p_B$, where $R > 1 > r \ge 0$ and $p_B \in (0,1)$. Furthermore, w.p. $\phi \in (0,1)$, it is feasible for the CEO to take a "good" decision, $d = G$, that is similar to $B$, except that the probability of receiving the high cash cash flow $R$ is higher, $p_G \in (p_B, 1]$. With probability $1 - \phi$, the decision $G$ is not feasible and the manager must choose between $S$ and $B$. We think of $S$ as a safe project, while $B$ and $G$ are risky growth options. Let $V_S = 1$, and for $d = B, G$, let $V_d = p_d R + (1 - p_d)r$. Assume $V_G > V_S > V_B$.

If the shareholders could enforce a decision, they would make the manager choose $G$ when feasible, and $S$ otherwise. However, the shareholders face a *hidden action* problem, in that they cannot observe the decision $d$ taken by the manager. Furthermore, they face a *hidden information* problem in that they do not observe whether $d = G$ is feasible or not. The two informational frictions together imply that shareholders do not know the feasible set of decisions available to the manager and which decision the manager actually took from his feasible set. They need to incentivize the manager to take the decisions they desire from a set of feasible decisions that is unknown to them. Assume that cash flows are verifiable in a court so that the compensation of the manager can be made contingent on realized cash flows.

To make incentive provision non-trivial, assume the manager earns (non-pecuniary) private benefit $b_d$ from decision $d \in \{S, B, G\}$ and normalize $b_S = 0$. Assume $b_B > \max\{0, b_G\}$. These private incentives may arise out of empire-building motives on the part of the manager or because of behavioral biases such as hubris or overconfidence.⁶ In the case $b_G < 0$, the manager's ranking of his private benefits from each decision is the opposite of the shareholders' ranking of total cash flows. Such directly opposed interests correspond to the standard cost of effort formulation of hidden action moral hazard problems. Generating higher expected cash flows involves more costly

the case where $u < 1$ so that it is profitable for the shareholders to implement the regime ng (and
so also g), absent any informational problems.

The manager has limited liability, i.e., he must receive non-negative payments. We assume that
the firm has enough cash to pay the manager and, accordingly, ignore limited liability constraints
on the firm side. As mentioned above, shareholders must also provide incentives for the manager
to choose the correct decision in each state of the world. Figure 1 denotes the timing of events.⁸

Shareholders must choose $W = (w_1, w_2, W_2)$ to maximize (2) subject to the following constraints:
$$
w_2 + p_G W_2 + b_G \ge w_2 + p_B W_2 + b_B \quad (3)
$$
$$
w_2 + p_G W_2 + b_G \ge w_1 \quad (4)
$$
$$
w_1 \ge w_2 + p_B W_2 + b_B \quad (5)
$$
$$
\phi(w_2 + p_G W_2 + b_G) + (1 - \phi)w_1 \ge u \quad (6)
$$
$$
w_1, w_2, W_2 + w_2 \ge 0 \quad (7)
$$

The first two constraints, (3) and (4), are hidden action incentive constraints. They ensure, respectively, that the manager prefers to undertake $G$ over $B$ and $G$ over $S$. Constraint (5) is another incentive constraint that arises out of the hidden information problem. It ensures the manager prefers $S$ over $B$ (when $G$ is not feasible). Together, the three incentive constraints guarantee that the manager will implement the regime $g$. Constraint (6) is the participation constraint that assures the manager an expected payoff of at least $u$. The limited liability constraints are captured by (7). Let $\omega_g = \frac{p_G b_B - p_B b_G}{p_G - p_B}$. As we show below, $\omega_g$ is the minimum possible payoff to the manager when the firm implements $g$.

**Proposition 1** *Suppose the shareholders wish to implement the first-best regime $g$. Then the date 0 expected shareholder value is*
$$
1 + \phi(V_G - 1 + b_G) - \max\{u, \omega_g\}.
$$

*A compensation contract that achieves this objective at minimum cost is given by $w_1 = \max\{u, \omega_g\}$, $w_2 = \max\{0, u - \omega_g\}$, $W_2 = \frac{b_B - b_G}{p_G - p_B}$.*

The proof of Proposition 1 shows that when $u < \omega_g$, the participation constraint (6) does not bind. Incentive and limited liability constraints determine managerial compensation. The manager's expected payoff is $\omega_g$, which gives him rents over his outside option $u$ even though he

[Page 10]
has no bargaining power and shareholders hold all rights over contracting.⁹ The optimal contract is unique, and a number of its features arise from the interaction of the hidden action and the hidden information problems faced by the shareholders.

First, the bonus $W_2$ is entirely determined by the choice between $G$ and $B$. The more similar are the private benefits of the manager for these two options, the lower is $W_2$; and the more similar $G$ is to $B$ (i.e., the closer $p_g$ is to $p_B$), the higher is $W_2$. This contracting feature creates adverse incentives for the manager: the bonus $W_2$ tempts the manager to choose $B$ over $S$ when $G$ is not feasible. To ensure the manager chooses $S$ when it is the best feasible alternative, $w_1$ must equal $w_g$. Like $W_2$, $w_g$ is larger the more difficult it is to distinguish between $G$ and $B$.

These properties of the optimal contract extend to situations where $u \ge w_g$ when the manager does not earn rents over his outside option, although in this case the cost-minimizing contract is not uniquely pinned down. Any contract that provides an expected payoff of $u$ to the manager and satisfies all the other constraints is an optimal contract. This indeterminacy has no effect on the expected level of managerial compensation and shareholder value. Proposition 1 provides the optimal contract that has the lowest bonus component in the class of all contracts that minimize the wage bill. The expected payoff of the manager equals $\max\{u, w_g\}$ in all cases.¹⁰

$$
\text{Pr}[\psi = H|G \text{ arrives}] = \text{Pr}[\psi = L|G \text{ does not arrive}] = \rho \in (\frac{1}{2}, 1]. \quad (8)
$$
The parameter $\rho$ measures the informativeness of the signal $\psi$. Denote by $P_{1,\psi}$ the date 1 stock price (or shareholder value) as a function of $\psi$. This equals expected total surplus less expected managerial compensation, given $\psi$. Denote by $E[W|\psi]$ the expected value of the managerial compensation package as a function of the signal $\psi$. Our next result shows that the random variables $P_{1,\psi}$ and $E[W|\psi]$ may display arbitrary correlation patterns, depending on the underlying agency conflict between the manager and shareholders.¹¹

## 2.2 Optimal regimes

So far, we have derived the optimal contract assuming that shareholders implement the first best regime g. We now provide the optimal contract when the shareholders decide to forego any growth option and induce the manager to always choose the safe option S. We call this regime ng. If implemented, the expected date 0 shareholder value is $1 - w_1$. The shareholders choose $W = (w_1, w_2, W_2)$ to maximize this subject to the following constraints
$$
w_1 \ge w_2 + p_B W_2 + b_B
$$
$$
w_1 \ge w_2 + p_G W_2 + b_G
$$
$$
w_1 \ge u
$$
$$
w_1, w_2, W_2 \ge 0
$$
The first two constraints are incentive constraints that ensure that the safe action is better for the manager than either the bad or the good action. The third constraint is the participation constraint, while the last one is the limited liability constraint. It is easy to check that a cost-minimizing way to implement ng is to set deferred compensation $w_2 = W_2 = 0$, and $w_1 = \max\{u, w_{ng}\}$ where, by analogy with Proposition 1, $w_{ng} \equiv b_B$ is the minimum managerial expected payoff (in this case, efficiency wage) associated with the regime ng.

Note that $w_{ng} < w_g$. It is less costly to provide incentives in regime ng than in regime g. This is firstly because the hidden information problem has any bite only in the latter case, in which the contract itself creates monetary incentives to take value-destroying decisions that must be mitigated by providing additional incentive pay. # 3 The Matching Market

We now embed our contracting problem into a model of frictionless matching between multiple
(potential) managers and firms. Managers differ in talent, firms in quality. Talent and quality are
observable and complementary inputs into the production of total surplus within a manager-firm
pair. In particular, talent is not firm-specific: it is portable across firms in a manner that depends
on firm quality.

As is well known, complementarities often generate increasing returns to managerial talent (and
firm quality) through positively assortative matching. As we show below, the two informational
frictions in our contracting problem—hidden action and hidden information—additionally generate
regime shifts within and across firms, leading to pay escalation accompanied by changes in firm
characteristics. Within-firm hidden information, in particular, is the key driver of these regime
shifts. The presence of both frictions distinguishes our analysis within the related literature on
matching with endogenous contracting. This literature either abstracts from informational frictions
(Becker 1973; Legros and Newman 2007; Gabaix and Landier 2008; Thanassoulis 2012; Chiappori
and Reny 2016; Gierlinger and Laczó 2018), or focuses on asymmetric information about talent
(Terviö 2008; Eisfeldt and Kuhnen 2013), or treats hidden action as the sole friction (Dam and
Pérez-Castrillo 2006; von Lilienfeld-Toal and Mookherjee 2010; Alonso-Paulí and Pérez-Castrillo

Let $f \in [0, \bar{f}]$ index a firm and $m \in [0, \bar{m}]$ index a manager. Let $P$ and $Q$ be the cumulative
distribution of firms and managers, respectively, with strictly positive densities $p$ and $q$. Set $P(\bar{f}) = \gamma > 0$ and $Q(\bar{m}) = 1$. When $\gamma = 1$, the market is *balanced*; otherwise it is *unbalanced*.

Firms and managers match in pairs. The characteristics of a match $(f, m)$ determine the
probability $\phi(f, m)$ of encountering the good growth option G. We assume $\phi$ is increasing and
differentiable in each argument and supermodular.[^13] Thus, manager $m$ is more talented than
$m' < m$ and firm $f$ has higher quality than $f' < f$. Further, talent and quality are complements—a
more talented manager will make a greater difference at a higher quality firm.

A match $(f, m) \in [0, \bar{f}] \times [0, \bar{m}]$ implements a regime $\theta(f, m) \in \{\text{g, ng}\}$ that delivers an expected payoff of $u_\theta(f,m)$ to the manager, $v_\theta(f, m)$ to the firm, with a total pie $\pi_\theta(f,m) = u_\theta(f,m) + v_\theta(f, m)$.¹⁴ To implement $\theta$, firm $f$ and manager $m$ write contracts satisfying the appropriate limited liability and incentive constraints, like the ones used to establish Propositions 1 through 3. Thus, $\pi_\text{g}(f,m) = 1+\phi(f,m)(V_G - 1+b_g)$, while $\pi_\text{ng}(f, m) = 1$. Let $\Delta_\pi(f, m) := \pi_\text{g}(f, m) – \pi_\text{ng}(f,m) = \kappa\phi(f,m)$ be the expected increase in the pie from implementing g in the match $(f,m)$, where $\kappa := V_G - 1 + b_g$.

A matching rule $\mu$ is a measurable one-to-one function $\mu : [0, \bar{m}] \to [0, \bar{f}] \cup \{-1\}$ that specifies the firm $\mu(m) \in [0, \bar{f}]$ that manager $m$ is matched with, or, if $m$ is unmatched, that $\mu(m) = -1$. Let $\mu^{-1}(f)$ denote the match of a firm $f$, with $\mu^{-1}(f) = -1$ if $f$ is unmatched, i.e., if $f \in [0, \bar{f}]\setminus\mu([0, \bar{m}])$. The rule $\mu$ must be measure-consistent, i.e., $\int_A dP = \int_{\mu^{-1}(A)} dQ$ for any measurable set $A \subseteq [0, \bar{f}]$.¹⁵

A matching is a tuple $(\mu,\theta,u,v)$ that consists of a matching rule $\mu$, and for every match $(\mu(m), m)$, $\mu(m) \neq -1$, a regime $\theta(\mu(m),m)$ that generates expected payoffs $u(\mu(m),m)$ and $v(\mu(m), m)$ to, respectively, the manager $m$ and his matched firm $\mu(m)$. An unmatched manager $m$ gets a payoff $u_0$, while an unmatched firm gets a payoff $v_0$. We further impose that if the market is balanced, and all managers are matched, then all firms are also matched, and vice-versa.¹⁶

A matching $(\mu, \theta, u, v)$ is individually rational if $u_\theta(\mu(m), m) \ge u_0$ and $v_\theta(f, \mu^{-1}(f)) \ge v_0$ for all $m$ and $f$ respectively. A pair $(f,m)$ blocks the matching $(\mu, \theta, u, v)$ if there exists a regime $\theta'$ that generates payoffs $u_{\theta'}(f,m) \ge u_\theta(\mu(m), m)$ and $v_{\theta'}(f, m) \ge v_\theta(f, \mu^{-1}(f))$, with at least one strict inequality. A matching $(\mu, \theta, u, v)$ is stable if it is individually rational and cannot be blocked by any $(f,m)$.

Our equilibrium notion is of a stable matching. We look for the set of all equilibria. For expositional ease, we restrict attention to environments with the following parameter restrictions:¹⁷

R1: $\phi(0,0) > 0$; $\phi(\bar{f},\bar{m}) > \phi^*(0)$.

R2: $b_B > u_0 > 0$.

R3: $\pi_\text{ng}(0,0) - \omega_\text{ng} > \pi_\text{g}(0,0) - \omega_\text{g} > v_0$.

Our next result shows that the multiplicity of stable matchings in a balanced market gives way to a unique stable matching, for any imbalance in the measures of potential firms and managers.

*Proposition 5*

*(i) Assume there are more firms than managers ($\gamma > 1$). The unique stable matching is the manager-optimal stable matching of a balanced market with firm distribution $P^+(f) = \max\{0, P(f) – (\gamma − 1)\}$ and manager distribution Q, with a measure $\gamma − 1$ of the lowest quality firms remaining unmatched.*

*(ii) Assume there are fewer firms than managers ($\gamma < 1$). The unique stable matching is the firm-optimal stable matching of a balanced market with firm distribution P and manager distribution $Q^+(m) = \max\{0, Q(m) – (1 − \gamma)\}$, with a measure $1 - \gamma$ of the least talented managers remaining unmatched.*

Proposition 5 is intuitive. When $\gamma > 1$ and there is a shortage of potential managers, the unique equilibrium is the manager-optimal stable matching of the balanced market in which a measure $\gamma - 1$ of the lowest quality firms have effectively been removed from the market. All managers and an equal measure of the highest quality firms match assortatively and implement g. The measure $\gamma - 1$ of the (removed) lowest quality firms remains unmatched. The lowest type of manager earns an expected payoff of $\pi_g(\mu(0), 0) - v_0$ that leaves its match indifferent between matching or staying unmatched. All higher types earn strictly higher payoffs according to (11). In contrast, when $\gamma < 1$ and there is an excess of potential managers, the unique equilibrium is the firm-optimal stable matching of the balanced market in which a measure $1 - \gamma$ of the least talented managers have

innovations, and regulatory reforms may have reduced historical barriers to firm formation. But
high costs of education and training, or other social constraints, may have prevented a corresponding
growth in the number of talented managers. If so, Proposition 5 predicts a sharp escalation in
executive compensation over time, accompanied by changes in firm characteristics due to regime
shifts.

What would happen for fixed sizes, 1 and $\gamma$, of the two sides of the market, but an improvement
in the distribution of types on one side? We focus on distribution improvements in the sense of
strict first-order stochastic dominance. Thus, $P'$ is an improvement over $P$ if $P'(f) < P(f)$ for each
$f \in (0, \bar{f})$; and similarly for the distribution $Q$ of managerial talent. We say that a distribution
change benefits a particular side of the market (manager or firm), if some agents on that side are
strictly better off and no one is strictly worse off. Similarly, the change harms a side of the market
if some agents on that side are strictly worse off and no one is strictly better off.

**Proposition 6** *Consider an unbalanced market ($\gamma \neq 1$). An improvement in the distribution of
one side of the market harms the same side and benefits the opposite side.*

Proposition 6 is a consequence of non-decreasing returns to managerial talent, which is a property of
stable matchings. It is easiest to see for an improvement in the distribution $P$ of firm qualities in a
market with $\gamma > 1$. Because of the shortage of managers, the unique equilibrium before the change
is the manager-optimal stable matching described by Proposition 5. After the change, measure
consistency implies each $m > m^* = 0$ is now matched with a better quality firm than before. So
each manager earns higher returns to managerial talent, as can be seen from expression (11). By
the same token, each matched firm is now matched to a less talented manager, implying the total
pie is smaller in its match. Proposition 6 is of a piece with our preceding results, because it shows
that an improvement over time in the quality distribution of firms, or a worsening of the talent
distribution of managers, may have contributed to the observed escalation in executive pay.²³

The proximate effect of $u_o$ is on managerial pay for the low risk firms in regime ng, which leads to a spillover effect on the set of high risk firms in regime g. Thus, if an econometrician only observed the subsample of risky firms with high incentive pay, it may not be easily apparent why a rise in $u_o$ affects anything, because each manager in his sample may have expected pay that is an order of magnitude greater than $u_o$.

Our model is consistent with these developments. It has two ingredients: the contracting
problem of Section 2, embedded in the matching market of Section 3. In the contracting problem,
a standard hidden action environment is enriched with hidden information: shareholders are unsure
which actions the manager can actually take. This single friction creates the possibility of regime
shifts, with discontinuous and simultaneous jumps in incentive pay and firm volatility, and a loose
link between pay and realized performance. The frictionless matching market features two-sided
vertical differentiation—firms differ in quality, managers differ in talent—and managerial skills are
portable across firms. Competitive forces then pin down the division of surplus, with no role for
within-firm bargaining power. The interaction of hidden information and competitive matching
generates our results on compensation patterns.

In equilibrium, the highest-quality firms must match with the most talented managers. For
some of these firms, inducing risky growth through high-powered incentives is a dominant strategy.
When technological or regulatory changes shift the distribution of opportunities upward, the set
of firms that prefer the growth regime expands. Lower quality firms paired with less talented
managers face an additional tension: while passing on growth opportunities is optimal for them
when managers are cheap, market-driven wage escalation can make inducing growth attractive,
even when such opportunities are very unlikely to materialize. This feedback loop—market wages
influencing firm regime choice leading to significantly higher incentive pay at most firms—plays a
central role in the empirical implications discussed below.

### Discontinuous jumps in compensation and volatility.
We predict large, abrupt increases
in CEO pay concentrated in firms that move from safe, low-growth strategies to risky, high-growth
ones. Smooth changes in economic conditions produce a sharp jump in incentive pay at the
(marginal) firm that switches regimes. Hidden information frictions are central here: they make
high expected pay unavoidable for firms choosing risky growth, regardless of the actual likelihood

**Disconnect between compensation and shareholder value.** The model yields two implications for the relationship between pay and shareholder value, one arising out of our contracting

# 5 Conclusion

We develop a competitive matching model for CEOs and firms that incorporates hidden action and
hidden information frictions. Our framework shows how regime shifts in executive compensation
are jointly determined with changes in firm risk profiles, explaining dramatic pay escalation post-
1980, and providing additional sharp testable implications. It also clarifies how high pay can result
from competitive forces even with an excess supply of managers, and why we may see large CEO
compensation even when the firm is struggling.

It is an interesting question whether the well-documented rise in executive compensation levels
is better explained by a race among firms for scarce managerial talent (superstars), or as a regime
shift common to most firms that leads to higher executive compensation associated with firms
choosing high-risk business strategies. We allow for both forces in our model. We find that the
effect of dispersed managerial talent on average executive compensation may well be small - after
all, everyone is unlikely to be a superstar - compared to the effect of a large majority of firms
switching from a low-risk no-growth regime to a high-risk surplus maximizing growth regime that
requires high-powered incentives. Most CEOs may earn high compensation even when they are not
superstars.

The evolution of CEO compensation is closely linked to fundamental shifts in global business.
Understanding the interaction between informational frictions and cascade effects can help explain
compensation dynamics that might otherwise appear baffling or unfair. Beyond direct competi-
tion for executives, cascades may also arise from product-market externalities generated by firms'
regime changes. Our model provides a workhorse framework for analyzing such network effects. As
industries transform rapidly—most notably with advances in AI-aggregate and industry-specific
shocks can trigger regime changes via the mechanisms we study.[^33] The same mechanisms can help
us understand episodes such as the dot-com boom and bust, and shifts in political support for
climate-change abatement.

This implies all firms will prefer the regime g, regardless of the value of $u$ or $\phi$. It rules out the possibility of regime shifts in our model, showing that hidden information is necessary for those results. For $\rho < 1$, the hidden information problem will still have some bite, so regime shifts are still possible, although the magnitude of the managerial payoff jump at the switching firm will become muted.

Clawback provisions are often observed for PE fund managers but are less frequent for managers of public firms. This may be because PE funds have a definite ending date, and clawbacks are usually triggered if the realized IRR at the funds' closing is less than some pre-specified benchmark. In contrast, strategic long-lived projects undertaken by the CEO of a publicly traded firm do not

[Page 41]
usually have a definite ending date by which the manager can be evaluated, perhaps making $\psi$
unreliable and clawbacks less attractive.

It is interesting to note, however, that even when feasible, contractual innovations such as
clawbacks may be entirely crowded out by competitive market forces. For instance, in the manager-
optimal stable matching (indeed, in any stable matching characterized in Proposition 4 with $m* =$
0), no firm can profitably offer such a contract, since all managers earn at least $w_g$. If we interpret
hidden information as a form of imperfect governance common across firms, this result implies
governance innovations themselves may be crowded out by market forces, an effect reminiscent of
the logic of Acharya and Volpin (2010).
