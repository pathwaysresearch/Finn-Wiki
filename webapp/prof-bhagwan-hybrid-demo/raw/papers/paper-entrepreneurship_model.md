# Scale, Delusion, and the Funding of Entrepreneurial Ventures

*A Model of Effort, Over-Optimism, and Welfare*

**Bhagwan Chowdhry**
*Indian School of Business*

**Nirvana Mitra**
*Indian School of Business*

March 2026 • Preliminary Draft

**Abstract.** We develop a model of entrepreneurial venture creation in which founders hold systematically upward-biased beliefs about project returns. A project of scale X succeeds with probability $\pi(e)$, increasing in effort, and yields KX; the entrepreneur believes the payoff is AKX where $\triangle > 1$. She raises external capital E in exchange for an equity stake, priced rationally by investors. The central result is that entrepreneurial delusion amplifies effort by exactly the factor A relative to the social optimum, generating a deadweight welfare loss proportional to $(\triangle-1)^2$ and to $X^2$. When project scale X is small, the effort cost outstrips actual output and funding destroys social value even though both the entrepreneur and investors willingly participate. When X is large, delusion serves as an endogenous participation-and-effort technology: it induces founders to bear the personal cost of building large, positive-NPV ventures that rational agents might not attempt. The model yields a sharp funding criterion: do not finance ventures below a minimum viable scale $X_{min}$ that is increasing in the degree of delusion A. The proliferation of micro-ventures in modern startup ecosystems—enabled by falling entry costs—may therefore represent large-scale misallocation of entrepreneurial effort.

**JEL Codes:** D84, G24, L26, M13 **Keywords:** entrepreneurship, over-optimism, delusion, venture capital, effort, welfare, scale

# 1. Introduction

Why do entrepreneurs work so hard on ventures that, in hindsight, were never worth building? The standard answer invokes information asymmetry, option value, or the simple fact that failure is more visible than the quiet success of not having tried. We propose a complementary explanation grounded in a different friction: founders are systematically deluded about the magnitude of their project's returns.

[Page 2]
This is not a novel empirical observation. A substantial literature documents that entrepreneurs
hold beliefs about their own success probabilities that far exceed actuarial base rates. Cooper, Woo,
and Dunkelberg (1988) found that 81% of founders rated their personal chances of success as 7
out of 10 or better, while only 33% assigned the same odds to a comparable business. Camerer and
Lovallo (1999) demonstrated in a controlled setting that excess entry into competitive markets is
driven by individuals overestimating their own relative skill. More recent work by Landier and
Thesmar (2009) formalizes the link between entrepreneurial optimism, financial contracts, and
capital misallocation.

What is less well understood is the interaction between the degree of delusion, the scale of the
venture, and social welfare. We show that this interaction is sharp and non-monotone. Delusion is
not uniformly bad: for large projects it serves as an effort-and-participation technology, inducing
founders to bear costs that rational agents would avoid. For small projects, however, the same
delusion becomes a trap-generating wasteful effort on ventures too small to justify it, even as
both the entrepreneur (who perceives high expected returns) and outside investors (who break even
by construction) participate voluntarily.

The model is deliberately spare. A project of scale X succeeds-yielding KX-with probability
$\pi(e)$, where e is the founder's effort. The founder believes the success payoff is $\Delta KX$, with $\Delta > 1$
a parameter of delusion. She raises capital E from competitive investors who price equity
rationally. Her cost of effort is $\delta e^2$. This three-parameter structure is sufficient to generate a
complete welfare analysis.

The key results are as follows. First, the deluded entrepreneur over-supplies effort by exactly the
factor $\Delta$, generating a deadweight loss of $(\Delta-1)^2AX^2$, where A is a scale-productivity constant.
Second, there exists a minimum viable scale $X^* = 1/A$ below which no project is socially
worthwhile even under first-best effort; delusion raises this threshold to $X_{min} = 1/[\Delta(2-\Delta)A]$,
strictly greater than $X^*$. The zone between $X^*$ and $X_{min}$ is a funding trap: the social planner
would fund, the entrepreneur eagerly participates, but actual welfare is negative. Third, for $\Delta \ge 2$,
no project of any scale is worth funding—extreme delusion is so effort-distorting that all surplus
is dissipated.

The empirical hook is sharp. Venture capital returns are famously right-skewed: a small fraction
of investments—almost always in large-market, capital-intensive ventures—account for the bulk

[Page 3]
of industry returns, while the median investment underperforms (Kaplan and Schoar, 2005; Harris,
Jenkinson, and Kaplan, 2014). Our model predicts exactly this pattern. The value of
entrepreneurial delusion is realized only at scale. Small-venture ecosystems, for all their vibrancy,
may represent the funding trap made systemic.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews related literature. Section 3
develops the model and derives equilibrium effort. Section 4 solves the social planner's problem
and characterizes the welfare wedge. Section 5 derives the funding geometry and main
propositions. Section 6 discusses empirical implications. Section 7 concludes.

# 2. Related Literature
Our paper sits at the intersection of three bodies of work: the economics of entrepreneurial over-
optimism, the theory of financial contracting under asymmetric beliefs, and the welfare analysis
of startup ecosystems.

## 2.1 Entrepreneurial Over-Optimism
The empirical evidence for entrepreneur over-optimism is extensive and robust across
methodologies and contexts. Survey evidence (Cooper, Woo, and Dunkelberg, 1988; Arabsheibani
et al., 2000) consistently finds that founders assign substantially higher success probabilities to
their own ventures than to comparable businesses. Experimental evidence (Camerer and Lovallo,
1999) shows that excess entry into competitive markets is driven by overconfidence about relative
skill, even when subjects are explicitly warned about the base rate. Longitudinal evidence from
matched datasets (Moskowitz and Vissing-Jørgensen, 2002) reveals that the average private equity
investor earns a risk-adjusted return below the public equity market, a puzzle most naturally
resolved by non-pecuniary motives or optimism bias.

We build on Landier and Thesmar (2009), who develop a contracting model in which an optimistic
entrepreneur and a rational investor sign a financial contract. They show that optimism can be
partially corrected through debt contracts (which penalize failure states that the entrepreneur
underweights) but not through equity. We differ in focusing on the interaction between delusion
and scale, and in deriving the social welfare implications of this interaction explicitly.

## 2.2 Financial Contracting and Asymmetric Beliefs

[Page 4]
The theoretical literature on financial contracting with heterogeneous beliefs (Geanakoplos, 2010;
Simsek, 2013; Fostel and Geanakoplos, 2015) focuses primarily on asset markets rather than the
entrepreneurial context. Harrison and Kreps (1978) established that speculative premia arise when
investors disagree and shorting is constrained—a structure that maps naturally onto the equity
financing of new ventures, where the entrepreneur cannot short her own optimism. Our model
adapts this logic to the effort-supply decision, showing that the entrepreneur's equity stake is not
merely mispriced but actively distorts her effort choice.

## 2.3 Scale and the Economics of Startups
The question of optimal startup size has been addressed from several angles. Lucas (1978)
develops a span-of-control model in which managerial talent determines firm size. Rajan and
Zingales (1998) show that access to external finance disproportionately benefits capital-intensive
industries. More recently, the ‘lean startup' movement (Ries, 2011) has argued, on grounds of
optionality and learning, that small initial scale is optimal for new ventures. Our model offers a
welfare-based counterargument: falling entry costs and the democratization of startup capital may
expand the set of active ventures into a region where delusion-driven effort destroys rather than
creates value.

# 3. The Model

## 3.1 Environment
Consider an entrepreneur who has access to a project requiring a total investment of X. We call X
the scale of the venture. If undertaken, the project succeeds with probability $\pi(e)$, where $e \ge 0$ is
the entrepreneur's effort and $\pi(\cdot)$ is twice differentiable, strictly increasing, and strictly concave,
with $\pi(0) = 0$ and $\pi'(0) = \infty$ (Inada condition). Success yields gross output KX with K > 1. Failure
yields zero. The project is thus a scaled Bernoulli: more scale magnifies both the upside and the
expected value of effort.

For closed-form tractability, we adopt throughout the linear specification:
$$
\pi(e) = ae, \quad a > 0, \quad ae \le 1.
$$

[Page 5]
Under this specification $\pi'(e) = a$ is constant. The qualitative results extend to any strictly concave
$\pi$ satisfying the Inada condition; the linear case gives exact, verifiable formulas.

## 3.2 The Entrepreneur
The entrepreneur contributes equity E from her own wealth and raises the residual X – E from
outside investors. She is characterized by a belief distortion parameter $\Delta > 1$: she perceives the
success payoff to be $\Delta KX$ rather than the true KX. This is a reduced-form representation of
optimism bias—she systematically overestimates the magnitude of the upside. All agents observe
$\Delta$; only the entrepreneur acts on it.

The entrepreneur's cost of effort is quadratic: $C(e) = \delta e^2$, with $\delta > 0$. This is standard in the effort-
supply literature and ensures an interior optimum. Her limited liability constraint is non-binding
throughout (she earns zero in failure regardless).

## 3.3 Investors
Outside investors are risk-neutral, competitive, and rational: they use the true probability $\pi(e)$ when
pricing securities. They receive an equity fraction $\phi$ of the success payoff. The investor
participation constraint (zero expected profit) pins down $\phi$:

$\pi(e) \cdot KX \cdot \Phi = X-E$
(ICInv)

so that $\phi = (X - E) / [\pi(e) KX]$.

The entrepreneur retains fraction $1 - \phi$. Note that investors price equity using true $\pi(e)$: there is no
scope for the entrepreneur to exploit investor credulity. The distortion is purely internal—it affects
her effort choice, not the terms of the contract.

## 3.4 The Entrepreneur's Perceived Problem
The entrepreneur maximizes her perceived utility, substituting $\Delta KX$ for KX:

$\max_e \pi(e) \cdot \Delta KX \cdot (1 - \phi) - \delta e^2 - E$

Substituting the expression for $\phi$ and simplifying:

[Page 6]
$= \Delta [ \pi(e)KX - X + E ] - \delta e^2 - E$
$= \Delta\{ (\pi(e)K - 1)X\} + (\Delta - 1)E - \delta e^2.$

Two terms drive participation. The first, $\Delta(\pi(e)K - 1)X$, is the deluded expected net surplus from the project. The second, $(\Delta - 1)E$, is a pure delusion bonus: the entrepreneur overvalues her own equity stake, lowering the effective cost of participation. This is the mechanism through which large $\Delta$ generates excess entry.

## 3.5 Equilibrium Effort
The first-order condition with respect to e under the linear specification $\pi(e) = ae$:
$$
\Delta \cdot aKX = 2\delta e^* \quad \Rightarrow \quad e^* = \Delta aKX / 2\delta
\quad (1)
$$
The deluded entrepreneur's effort $e^*$ is linear in scale X, increasing in delusion $\Delta$, and decreasing in effort cost $\delta$. The dependence on X is the key scaling result: large projects generate high equilibrium effort, compounding both the upside (higher $\pi(e^*) \cdot KX$) and the distortion ($\delta(e^*)^2$ grows quadratically in X).

# 4. Welfare Analysis

## 4.1 First-Best: The Social Planner's Problem
A benevolent social planner chooses effort to maximize true total surplus, using the correct probability $\pi(e)$ and setting $\Delta = 1$:
$$
\max_e W(e) = ae \cdot KX - X - \delta e^2
$$
The first-order condition yields first-best effort and welfare:
$$
e^{fb} = aKX / 2\delta
\quad (2)
$$
$$
W^{fb} = (a^2K^2 / 4\delta) X^2 - X = AX^2 - X
\quad (3)
$$
where we define $A = a^2K^2 / 4\delta$ as the scale productivity constant. The first-best project is socially viable if and only if $W^{fb} \ge 0$, i.e., if project scale meets the threshold:

[Page 7]
$X \ge X^* = 1/A = 4\delta / \alpha^2 K^2$.
(4)
Below X*, no project is worth undertaking even under optimal effort. X* is the floor of the
economically relevant scale range.

## 4.2 Actual Welfare Under Delusion
Under the deluded equilibrium, the entrepreneur supplies $e^* = \Delta e^{fl}$. Substituting into the true
welfare function:
$W^{actuaL} = ae^* \cdot KX – X – \delta(e^*)^2$
$= \Delta(2 − \Delta) \cdot AX^2 – X$.
The factor $\Delta(2 – \Delta) \in (−1, 1]$ for $\Delta \ge 1$ is a delusion discount applied to the productive term. At $\Delta$
= 1 it equals 1 (no distortion); at $\Delta = 2$ it equals 0; for $\Delta > 2$ it is negative.

## 4.3 The Welfare Wedge
The deadweight loss from entrepreneurial delusion is:
$W^{fl} – W^{actuaL} = (\Delta − 1)^2 \cdot AX^2$.
(6)
This is the paper's central formula. Three features deserve emphasis.

**Convexity in delusion.** The loss grows as $(\Delta−1)^2$, not linearly. A little delusion ($\Delta$ close to 1)
generates a small distortion; substantial delusion ($\Delta = 1.5$, say) generates four times the loss of
modest delusion ($\Delta = 1.25$).

**Convexity in scale.** The loss scales as $X^2$, growing faster than the first-best surplus (also $AX^2$ in
the productive term, but net of a linear cost). This means delusion is relatively more damaging for
large projects in absolute terms but may still be dominated by the large scale benefit.

**Zero at $\Delta = 1$.** In the absence of delusion there is no distortion—trivially, but the formula confirms
the model is internally consistent.

# 5. The Funding Geometry

[Page 8]
## 5.1 Three Regimes
Define the minimum viable scale under delusion as the scale at which $W^{actual} = 0$:
$X_{min} = 1/[\Delta(2-\Delta) \cdot A] = 4\delta / [\Delta(2-\Delta)\alpha^2K^2]$.
(7)

Since $\Delta(2-\Delta) < 1$ for all $\Delta > 1$, we have $X_{min} > X^*$. Three regimes partition the scale space:

**Regime I:** $X < X^*$. No project is viable even in the first best. Trivially, do not fund.

**Regime II:** $X^* \le X < X_{min}$. The social planner would fund ($W^{fl} > 0$) but actual welfare is negative ($W^{actual} < 0$). This is the Funding Trap. Do not fund.

**Regime III:** $X \ge X_{min}$. Both first-best and actual welfare are non-negative. Fund.

The width of the Funding Trap is:
$X_{min} - X^* = (4\delta / \alpha^2K^2) \cdot (\Delta-1)^2 / [\Delta(2-\Delta)]$,
which is increasing in $(\Delta-1)^2$ and decreasing in $K^2$. High-productivity sectors ($\alpha$ and $K$ large) have narrow traps; sectors prone to deep delusion have wide ones.

## 5.2 The Critical Delusion Threshold
As $\Delta \to 2$, $\Delta(2-\Delta) \to 0$ and $X_{min} \to \infty$: no finite-scale project survives. For $\Delta \ge 2$, $W^{actual} \le -X < 0$ always. The entrepreneur is not merely over-working—she is destroying value faster than the investment itself. Never fund.

## 5.3 The Funding Trap and the Role of Enthusiasm
The Funding Trap is not a failure of rational pricing. Investors break even by assumption—they earn exactly $X - E$ in expectation. The entrepreneur participates voluntarily—she perceives positive surplus from the $(\Delta-1)E$ bonus term. Yet society loses. This is the sense in which entrepreneurial enthusiasm can be self-defeating at small scale: the very eagerness of the founder signals misallocated effort, not genuine opportunity.

Compare this to a model of pure moral hazard without delusion ($\Delta = 1$). There, the entrepreneur under-supplies effort (because she bears the full cost but shares the upside with investors).

[Page 9]
Delusion reverses this: the entrepreneur over-supplies effort by convincing herself the upside is
larger than it is. Both distortions are first-order in magnitude; they operate in opposite directions;
and neither is visible to investors who simply price whatever effort the entrepreneur supplies.

# 6. Main Results

**Proposition 1 (Effort Distortion).** The deluded entrepreneur supplies effort $e* = \Delta \cdot e^{fl}$,
overworking relative to the social optimum by the factor $\Delta$. The excess effort $(\Delta-1)e^{fl}$ is entirely
wasteful.

**Proposition 2 (Welfare Loss).** The deadweight loss from entrepreneurial delusion equals
$(\Delta-1)^2AX^2$. It is convex in the degree of delusion and convex in project scale.

**Proposition 3 (Funding Threshold).** For $1 < \Delta < 2$, the minimum socially viable scale is $X_{min}
= 4\delta / [\Delta(2-\Delta)\alpha^2K^2] > X*$. Projects with $X < X_{min}$ destroy social value despite voluntary
participation by both founders and investors.

**Proposition 4 (Critical Delusion).** For $\Delta \ge 2$, no project is worth funding regardless of scale.
Extreme delusion dissipates all surplus through excess effort costs.

**Proposition 5 (Delusion as Participation Technology).** For $X \ge X_{min}$, the $(\Delta-1)E$
participation bonus enables founders to bear the personal cost of large, positive-NPV ventures.
The social gain from this exceeds the welfare loss from over-effort when $X$ is sufficiently large
relative to $X_{min}$.

# 7. Empirical Implications

## 7.1 Venture Capital Return Distributions

The model's most direct empirical prediction concerns the distribution of returns to venture
investment. Proposition 3 implies that value creation is concentrated in Regime III ($X \ge X_{min}$):
smaller ventures lie in the Funding Trap and generate negative actual welfare. This maps to the
well-documented extreme right-skewness of VC returns: Kaplan and Schoar (2005) document that
the top quartile of VC funds generates nearly all industry returns, while the median fund
underperforms public equity. Harris, Jenkinson, and Kaplan (2014) confirm this pattern in a dataset
of 1,400 U.S. buyout and venture funds. The model predicts that this skewness should be more
pronounced in industries with higher $\Delta$ (consumer internet, social media) and less pronounced in
sectors where delusion is disciplined by tangible milestones (biotech, semiconductors).

## 7.2 The Lean Startup Paradox

[Page 10]
The ‘lean startup' philosophy (Ries, 2011) advocates for small initial scale as a means of reducing
learning costs and preserving option value. Our model suggests a welfare caveat: if reducing initial
scale pushes a venture from Regime III into the Funding Trap, the option value of learning is
purchased at the cost of negative expected social welfare. Lean startup logic is sound for ventures
where X can be grown cheaply once the market is validated (i.e., where K is learned by doing and
$\alpha$ rises with experience). It is potentially dangerous for ventures where scale is a prerequisite for
any output at all—network-effect businesses, infrastructure, advanced manufacturing.

## 7.3 Startup Failure Rates and Effort Waste
The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics reports that approximately 45% of new businesses fail within
five years and roughly 65% within ten years. Standard models attribute this to adverse selection
(bad entrepreneurs enter) or learning (entrepreneurs discover their type). Our model adds a third
channel: some businesses fail not because the entrepreneur was untalented or unlucky, but because
she was too talented—she worked so hard, driven by $\Delta > 1$, that the effort cost consumed all
potential surplus on a project too small to justify it. This predicts that high-effort founders of small
ventures should exhibit lower ex post welfare than low-effort founders of comparably sized
ventures, controlling for outcomes.

## 7.4 Testing the Scale Threshold
The model yields a testable structural prediction: there should be a discontinuity in social welfare
(proxied by value creation, employment generated, or follow-on financing) around $X_{min}$.
Ventures just above this threshold should exhibit a sharp improvement in outcomes relative to
those just below it. Identifying $X_{min}$ empirically requires an estimate of the triple $(\alpha, K, \delta)$, which
could in principle be estimated from data on effort inputs (hours worked, founder salaries
foregone), success rates, and output magnitudes across venture cohorts. This remains an avenue
for future empirical work.

# 8. Conclusion
We have developed a model showing that entrepreneurial delusion ($\Delta > 1$) is neither uniformly
good nor uniformly bad. It is a conditional blessing: beneficial when it induces founders to build

[Page 11]
large, genuinely productive ventures that rational agents would not attempt; harmful when it drives
effort into projects too small to generate positive actual welfare. The deadweight loss from
delusion is convex in both the degree of distortion and the project scale, and the minimum viable
scale rises with Δ.

The policy implication is pointed. Much of the institutional apparatus of modern startup
ecosystems—incubators, accelerators, angel networks, micro-VC funds—is designed to lower the
barriers to starting small. Our model suggests that lowering the barrier to small ventures may
expand the Funding Trap rather than the value-creation zone. The question is not whether more
entrepreneurship is better, but whether the marginal venture—the one that exists only because
entry costs have fallen—lies in Regime II or Regime III. If it lies in Regime II, funding it is a
transfer from entrepreneurs (who bear effort costs in excess of their actual welfare) to investors
(who break even) and ultimately destroys social value.

Three extensions are natural. First, a dynamic model in which Δ is learned over time: if founders
update their beliefs when early signals arrive, the optimal contract may front-load payments to
preserve learning incentives. Second, a general equilibrium treatment in which the supply of
deluded founders is endogenous to the entry cost—falling entry costs may not merely expand the
set of ventures but shift the distribution of Δ upward by attracting founders who would have been
deterred by the rational calculus. Third, a calibration exercise mapping X* and X_min to actual
funding thresholds in identified venture markets.

The deeper point is perhaps this: entrepreneurship is not a free good. Founders pay for it with their
time, their capital, and—when delusion is involved—with their welfare. We owe it to them, and
to ourselves, to fund only those ventures large enough to justify what they cost.
