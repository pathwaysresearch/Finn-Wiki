# Stock Options

Stock options are contracts that give the holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset at a predetermined price before a specified expiration date. Because they are created in equal and opposite amounts (a long position matched by a short position), they are zero‑net‑supply instruments in financial markets. This same contractual feature also makes them powerful asymmetric incentive tools when granted to executives: the payoff is zero if the stock price stays below the strike, but rises linearly above it, linking a portion of compensation to the firm’s stock price and encouraging managers to pursue risky, growth‑oriented projects. The option‑pricing logic—rooted in models such as Black‑Scholes—that values these contracts as contingent claims applies unchanged whether the option is traded on an exchange or used as a component of executive compensation. Consequently, the dual role of stock options as market instruments and as governance mechanisms generates the empirically observed pay‑volatility co‑movement: periods of high executive compensation coincide with high firm volatility as firms shift between safe and risky strategies.
