# Stock Options

Stock options are financial contracts that give the holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset at a predetermined price before a specified expiration date. This asymmetric payoff structure makes them useful in two distinct contexts:

1. **Market Instruments** – As standardized derivatives traded on exchanges, stock options provide leverage and convey information about the full distribution of future returns, especially tail risks. Their pricing relies on models like Black‑Scholes, and they can be combined to create any desired payoff profile.

2. **Corporate Incentives** – When granted to executives, stock options act as high‑powered, equity‑linked compensation. By linking a portion of pay to the firm’s stock price, they motivate managers to pursue risky, growth‑oriented projects. This dual role creates the empirically observed **pay‑volatility co‑movement**: periods of high executive compensation coincide with high firm volatility as firms adopt growth regimes requiring strong incentives.

Thus, the same contractual feature—right without obligation—underlies both the use of options as market derivatives and their function as governance tools.
